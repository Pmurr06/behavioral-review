The Effect of Cannabis on Schizophrenia Diagnoses

Ryan Trudeau

Public Health
University of Utah

Public Health July 2026 15 min read

Abstract

As high-potency cannabis products such as dab pens become increasingly popular, questions surrounding their long-term effects on mental health have grown more urgent. While cannabis legalization has expanded across much of the United States, emerging evidence suggests that products containing extremely high concentrations of tetrahydrocannabinol (THC) may significantly increase the risk of cannabis-induced psychosis and accelerate the onset of schizophrenia among genetically susceptible individuals. This article examines the relationship between dab pens, cannabis-induced psychosis, and schizophrenia by reviewing recent psychiatric and public health research. Although cannabis does not appear to independently cause schizophrenia, current evidence indicates that repeated exposure to high-potency THC products may worsen psychiatric outcomes and contribute to earlier disease onset in vulnerable populations. These findings highlight the importance of public education, continued research, and evidence-based public health policies as cannabis products continue to evolve.

Keywords

Cannabis, THC, Dab Pens, Schizophrenia, Cannabis-Induced Psychosis, Mental Health, Public Health, Substance Use

Introduction

Cannabis legalization remains a topic of ongoing debate throughout the United States, making it increasingly important to understand the different forms of cannabis and their potential health effects. Whether used medically or recreationally, marijuana has been legalized in many states and countries with the goal of providing pain relief, stress management, and other therapeutic benefits when used responsibly. In recent years, however, a new method of cannabis consumption—the dab pen, a portable vaporizing device capable of delivering extremely high concentrations of tetrahydrocannabinol (THC)—has rapidly grown in popularity. Their convenience, discreet design, and high potency have made dab pens particularly attractive to younger users.

Emerging research suggests that these high-potency cannabis products may carry substantially greater mental health risks than traditional forms of marijuana, particularly through their association with cannabis-induced psychosis and an increased likelihood of developing schizophrenia in vulnerable individuals. As these products become more common, understanding their potential consequences becomes increasingly important for both public health professionals and policymakers.

This paper examines the relationship between high-potency cannabis products and schizophrenia by differentiating between various forms of cannabis, evaluating recent research on dab pens, and exploring how repeated cannabis-induced psychotic episodes may contribute to chronic mental illness. Specifically, it seeks to answer the question: What are the consequences of dab pens on the mental health of marijuana users?

Before addressing this question, it is necessary to understand how the cannabis market has evolved in recent years. Although marijuana itself has been extensively studied for decades, dab pens represent a relatively new development, and research surrounding their long-term effects is still emerging. Similar to the rapid rise of nicotine vaping products during the twenty-first century, cannabis consumption has increasingly shifted toward vaporization. This trend has introduced cannabis products containing THC concentrations far exceeding those found in traditional marijuana flower, raising new concerns about their impact on mental health.

Considering these developments, it is crucial that high-potency cannabis products receive greater public health attention. Through existing research on vaping products and the growing body of literature surrounding dab pens, it becomes evident that stronger regulation, improved public education, and increased access to mental health resources may help reduce the risk of persistent psychotic disorders among vulnerable populations. To better understand these risks, this paper will examine dab pens, cannabis-induced psychosis, schizophrenia, and the complex relationship that connects them.

Dab Pens

Dab pens have become one of the fastest-growing products in the cannabis market. As of 2022, the most popular cannabis categories included traditional flower at 40.5% of sales, vapor pens at 26.3%, and pre-rolls at 12.3% (Jackson). Among these products, vapor pens containing highly concentrated cannabis oil have experienced particularly rapid growth because they provide a more intense and longer-lasting high than traditional marijuana. Jackson explains that “even in a tough macroeconomic climate, we've seen total sales, market share, and brand count grow across most markets,” demonstrating the continued expansion of vapor pen sales across the cannabis industry.

This growth is reflected in both the United States and Canada. In 2022, vapor pen sales in the United States increased by 23.3%, while the number of vapor pen brands available in Canada grew by 84.5% between 2021 and 2022 (Headset). Consumer trends further highlight this shift. According to Headset, Generation Z and Millennials account for 62.8% of all cannabis purchases and an even larger 70.8% of vapor pen sales. These statistics indicate that younger consumers are increasingly choosing high-potency cannabis products over more traditional forms of marijuana.

Understanding who primarily uses dab pens is essential when considering their potential public health implications. Research shows that young males make up the largest share of vapor pen consumers (Headset). Jackson also predicts that “we'll likely see continued growth in the future as a younger generation of consumers ages into the market.” As legalization continues to expand and high-potency cannabis products become more accessible, this trend raises important concerns for public health professionals. The increasing popularity of dab pens among younger users makes it critical to better understand the potential mental health consequences associated with repeated exposure to highly concentrated THC products.

Substance-Induced Psychosis

Marijuana is a form of cannabis that contains tetrahydrocannabinol (THC), the primary psychoactive compound responsible for many of the drug's effects. THC can alter mood, perception, and cognition while also increasing dopamine synthesis and blocking dopamine reuptake, creating what researchers describe as a hyperdopaminergic state (Sharma 322). In other words, THC increases the availability of dopamine—the neurotransmitter associated with pleasure and reward—producing feelings of euphoria and relaxation. These rewarding effects contribute to marijuana's addictive potential, particularly among adolescents and young adults whose brains are still developing.

Research supports this concern. More than 70% of adult daily cannabis users report beginning regular use before the age of eighteen (Padhi 132). Additionally, 22% of individuals studied had a family history of drug dependence, suggesting that both genetic and environmental factors may increase the likelihood of developing cannabis dependence. Early and repeated cannabis use can therefore create long-term health risks that extend beyond addiction itself.

As cannabis becomes more widely available through legalization, it is increasingly important to understand how cannabis dependence may contribute to serious psychiatric conditions. One of the most significant concerns is substance-induced psychosis (SIP), a psychotic state that occurs following the use of a psychoactive substance or during withdrawal and persists for more than forty-eight hours (Maes 45). When triggered specifically by marijuana, this condition is commonly referred to as cannabis-induced psychosis (CIP).

Unlike schizophrenia, CIP often resolves after cannabis use stops. However, growing evidence suggests that repeated psychotic episodes—particularly those involving high-potency cannabis—may increase the risk of developing persistent psychosis. Ricci reports that cannabis-induced psychosis progresses to persistent psychotic disorders in approximately 25–50% of cases involving repeated episodes or prolonged exposure to high doses of THC (457).

A case study illustrates how this progression can occur. Sharma describes a 21-year-old man with no family history of psychiatric illness who began smoking two to three marijuana joints each day. Over time, he experienced hallucinations, declining academic performance, insomnia, and difficulty concentrating. After quitting cannabis, his symptoms improved. However, when he resumed smoking at an even heavier rate—five to seven joints daily—he developed severe delusions, believing others were manipulating his brain waves and sabotaging his academic performance (322). This pattern demonstrates how escalating cannabis use may intensify psychotic symptoms in susceptible individuals.

Ricci describes this deterioration as a “lysergic psychoma,” in which individuals perceive an external force invading their own thoughts. According to Ricci, prolonged disruption of normal cognitive processing alters an individual's perception of time, space, and reality, making psychotic experiences increasingly difficult to distinguish from reality. He further characterizes this progression as a “twilight state,” a transitional phase in which repeated substance-induced psychosis gradually evolves into persistent psychotic illness.

Ultimately, cannabis serves as an important environmental risk factor for psychosis. Repeated exposure to high concentrations of THC appears to reduce an individual's ability to resist psychotic episodes, increasing vulnerability to chronic psychiatric disorders (Sharma 322). This concern becomes even more significant when considering how dramatically cannabis potency has changed over time. Between 1975 and 2017, average THC concentrations in cannabis products increased by approximately 0.57% each year (Maes 46). Modern dab pens contain THC concentrations ranging from 50% to 95%, far exceeding the potency of traditional marijuana flower.

Dr. Chad Percifield emphasizes this concern, reporting that THC vaping solutions increase the risk of psychotic disorders more than sixfold because vaporized cannabis products average 52% THC, compared to roughly 13% THC in traditional marijuana flower. This trend is not limited to the United States. In the United Kingdom, a high-potency cannabis strain known as “skunk,” containing up to 39% THC, now accounts for approximately 96% of London's street cannabis market (Quattrone 1329).

The growing popularity of high-potency cannabis products among younger users, combined with continued legalization efforts, has created conditions in which repeated cannabis-induced psychosis may become increasingly common. As THC concentrations continue to rise, understanding the relationship between cannabis use and persistent mental illness becomes an increasingly urgent public health priority.

Schizophrenia

Unlike cannabis-induced psychosis, schizophrenia is a chronic psychiatric disorder characterized by persistent changes in brain structure and function (Maes 45). According to current diagnostic criteria, schizophrenia is identified by the presence of at least two of five primary symptoms: delusions, hallucinations, disorganized speech, grossly disorganized or catatonic behavior, and negative symptoms such as diminished emotional expression or lack of motivation (Maes 45). These symptoms can significantly impair an individual's ability to function socially, academically, and professionally.

Although schizophrenia is strongly influenced by genetics, possessing a genetic predisposition alone does not guarantee that the disorder will develop. Instead, researchers believe schizophrenia results from a complex interaction between inherited genetic factors and environmental influences. Because the disorder is difficult to replicate in animal models, many aspects of its underlying biology remain poorly understood. Even so, it is estimated that approximately 1 in every 200 people will develop schizophrenia during their lifetime (Maes 45).

Researchers have identified several factors that increase an individual's risk of developing the disorder. Men are diagnosed with schizophrenia more frequently than women, and age is among the strongest predictors of onset. Most diagnoses occur during late adolescence through the early thirties, a developmental period when the brain is still undergoing important structural changes (Maes 47). Family history is another significant predictor. In a cross-sectional study conducted in India, Padhi found that 56% of participants in the schizophrenia control group had a family history of the disorder, highlighting the importance of inherited risk factors.

These risk factors—including age, sex, and genetics—are largely beyond an individual's control. However, research increasingly suggests that environmental exposures may influence whether an underlying genetic vulnerability is expressed. One environmental factor receiving growing attention is repeated episodes of cannabis-induced psychosis (Ricci 458). While cannabis does not appear to create schizophrenia in individuals without a predisposition to the disorder, evidence suggests that repeated psychotic episodes associated with high-potency cannabis use may accelerate or worsen the development of schizophrenia among those who are already genetically susceptible. This relationship has become a central focus of recent psychiatric and public health research.

Correlation Between Schizophrenia and Cannabis-Induced Psychosis

The relationship between cannabis use and schizophrenia is complex and continues to be an active area of psychiatric research. As Ricci notes, “comprehensive understanding of the psychopathological manifestations of substance abuse is limited and fragmented among health care professionals.” Although many questions remain unanswered, current evidence consistently suggests that cannabis-induced psychosis (CIP) increases the likelihood of developing schizophrenia among individuals who are already genetically vulnerable.

One of the strongest findings comes from an eight-year cohort study, which found that 46% of patients who experienced cannabis-induced psychosis eventually developed schizophrenia (Maes 46). Even more striking, researchers observed that while the transition from CIP to schizophrenia typically occurs over approximately thirteen years, individuals who repeatedly used high-potency cannabis often developed schizophrenia in as little as two years. These findings suggest that frequent exposure to highly concentrated THC may accelerate the onset of schizophrenia in susceptible individuals.

Researchers believe this progression may be linked to changes in brain structure. Studies have found that individuals with schizophrenia who regularly use cannabis exhibit greater structural brain abnormalities—including enlarged left and right ventricular volumes—than individuals with schizophrenia who do not use cannabis (Maes 48). In other words, cannabis may worsen neurological changes already associated with the disorder rather than creating an entirely new disease process.

Genetics may also help explain why some cannabis users develop schizophrenia while many others do not. One genetic variation, the Val158Met polymorphism, has been associated with schizophrenia and appears more frequently among cannabis users who experience psychotic episodes. In one study of 135 patients with schizophrenia, nearly 30% possessed this polymorphism (Maes 48). Because THC can trigger episodes of psychosis, researchers believe individuals carrying this genetic variation may be especially vulnerable to developing schizophrenia after repeated exposure to high-potency cannabis.

Additional evidence comes from a large Danish longitudinal study that followed 6,788 individuals between 1994 and 2014 who had experienced substance-induced psychosis, with cannabis being the most commonly used substance. Researchers found that 26% eventually developed schizophrenia (Maes 47). Compared with the estimated lifetime prevalence of schizophrenia—approximately 1 in 200 people—this represents a dramatically elevated risk. The study also found that 8.4% of participants later developed bipolar disorder, suggesting that cannabis-induced psychosis may contribute to the development of multiple chronic psychiatric illnesses.

Researchers estimate that 8–13% of schizophrenia cases may be attributable to cannabis use, with the risk increasing substantially when cannabis use begins before the age of fifteen (Maes 48). Young men appear to be particularly vulnerable. A study published by the National Institutes of Health reported that 30% of schizophrenia cases among men aged 21–30 might have been prevented by avoiding cannabis use disorder. The same research estimated that 15% of schizophrenia cases among men aged 16–49 in 2021 could have been prevented if cannabis use disorder had been avoided.

Taken together, this growing body of evidence suggests that repeated cannabis-induced psychosis can significantly increase the likelihood of developing schizophrenia among genetically predisposed individuals. As high-potency cannabis products such as dab pens become increasingly common, understanding this relationship is becoming an important public health priority. While cannabis may not independently cause schizophrenia, it appears capable of accelerating its onset and worsening its severity in individuals who are already biologically susceptible.

Alternative View

Although growing evidence links cannabis use with an increased risk of schizophrenia, researchers caution that the relationship is not purely causal. An important alternative perspective is that individuals who develop schizophrenia following cannabis-induced psychosis may already possess the same underlying genetic vulnerability as those who develop the disorder without cannabis use (Sharma 322). From this viewpoint, cannabis does not create schizophrenia in an otherwise healthy individual but instead acts as a trigger that accelerates or intensifies the progression of a disorder for which a person is already biologically predisposed.

This distinction is important because it helps explain why the vast majority of cannabis users never develop schizophrenia. Genetic susceptibility, family history, age, and other environmental influences all interact in complex ways, making it difficult to isolate cannabis as the sole cause of the disorder.

Nevertheless, the absence of a direct causal relationship does not eliminate the public health concern. As Maes argues, cannabis use is associated with a poorer prognosis among individuals with schizophrenia, contributing to earlier onset, more severe symptoms, and worse long-term outcomes. In other words, while cannabis may not independently cause schizophrenia, repeated exposure to high-potency cannabis can substantially increase the likelihood that a genetically vulnerable individual develops the disorder sooner and experiences more severe psychiatric complications.

Recognizing this distinction allows researchers and policymakers to approach cannabis use with greater nuance. Rather than suggesting that cannabis inevitably causes schizophrenia, the evidence indicates that high-potency cannabis products present a significant risk for individuals who are already predisposed to psychotic disorders. This understanding supports public health strategies focused on education, early screening, and targeted prevention for populations at the greatest risk.

Conclusion

The growing legalization of cannabis has reshaped public attitudes toward marijuana, often leading many people to view it as relatively harmless. While cannabis can provide legitimate medical benefits and is widely used for recreational purposes, the rapid rise of high-potency products such as dab pens presents new public health challenges that deserve greater attention. As legislators continue to expand access to cannabis, it is essential that policy decisions reflect not only the potential benefits of legalization but also the emerging evidence surrounding its risks.

One of the most significant concerns is the widespread availability of cannabis products containing exceptionally high concentrations of THC. As lead author Carsten Hjorthøj notes, increasing legalization has made cannabis one of the most commonly used psychoactive substances in the world while simultaneously reducing public perceptions of its potential harm. This changing perception is particularly concerning given the growing popularity of dab pens among adolescents and young adults—the populations shown to be at the greatest risk for cannabis-induced psychosis and later development of schizophrenia.

Public health officials have responded to the dangers of tobacco use and nicotine vaping through education campaigns, warning labels, and prevention initiatives. Similar efforts should be considered for high-potency cannabis products. Increasing public awareness of the relationship between repeated cannabis-induced psychosis and chronic mental illness may help individuals make more informed decisions about cannabis use, particularly those with known genetic or familial risk factors for psychiatric disorders.

Addressing this issue will require a multifaceted public health approach. Continued research into high-potency cannabis products, targeted education campaigns, screening for cannabis use disorder, and thoughtful regulation of THC concentrations may all help reduce the burden of cannabis-related mental illness. These efforts should focus especially on protecting adolescents and young adults, whose developing brains may be particularly vulnerable to the effects of repeated exposure to highly concentrated THC.

Ultimately, the evidence suggests that while cannabis does not independently cause schizophrenia, repeated use of high-potency products such as dab pens may substantially increase the risk of persistent psychosis and accelerate the onset of schizophrenia among genetically susceptible individuals. As cannabis legalization continues to evolve, public health policy should evolve alongside it, ensuring that education, prevention, and evidence-based regulation remain central to protecting vulnerable populations from potentially lifelong psychiatric consequences.
