Obstetric Paternalism in Italy: Autonomy, Medicalization, and the Ethics of Childbirth

Ryan Trudeau

Public Health
University of Utah

Public Health July 2026 10 min read

Abstract

This paper examines the case of Alessandra Battisti, an Italian woman who underwent an unwanted cesarean section in 2011 after medical staff allegedly proceeded without her informed consent. Using her experience as a foundation, this article analyzes the ethical conflict between patient autonomy and medical beneficence that underlies obstetric paternalism in Italy. Drawing on documented reports from the Obstetric Violence Observatory and the World Health Organization, the paper contextualizes Battisti’s case within a broader pattern of obstetric violence affecting approximately 21% of Italian mothers between 2006 and 2018. The article evaluates the ethical dimensions of strong paternalism—the overriding of a competent patient’s expressed wishes—through a deontological framework that prioritizes individual dignity, informed consent, and respect for persons as moral agents. The analysis concludes that the actions of Battisti’s medical team were ethically impermissible, as compelling clinical beneficence does not justify disregarding a patient’s right to autonomous decision-making. The paper ultimately argues that respecting informed consent is not merely a legal requirement but a foundational ethical obligation essential to maintaining trust, dignity, and collaboration in obstetric care.

Keywords

Bioethics, Medical Ethics, Patient Autonomy, Women’s Health, Obstetric Violence, Human Rights, Italy, Childbirth, Health Humanities, Public Health

Introduction

In 2011, Alessandra Battisti entered a hospital in Rome experiencing normal contractions, prepared to give birth to her daughter. Shortly after arriving, medical staff administered oxytocin to accelerate labor. Battisti later claimed the medication was given without explanation or informed consent. As labor progressed, physicians began preparing her for a cesarean section. Battisti repeatedly asked why the procedure was necessary and requested to continue with a natural birth. According to her account, her questions were ignored, and medical staff instead informed her that there were “signs of fetal suffering” before proceeding with the surgery against her wishes. The unexpected cesarean section left Battisti with lasting physical and emotional trauma (Cicali, 2018).

Following her discharge, Battisti pursued legal action against the hospital, becoming an advocate for women who had experienced similar treatment during childbirth. Her case brought national attention to the issue of obstetric violence, a form of institutional, gender-based violence committed against pregnant women by healthcare professionals.

Unfortunately, Battisti’s experience is not an isolated incident. Between 2006 and 2018, approximately 21% of mothers in Italy reported experiencing obstetric violence during childbirth (Cicali, 2018). Researchers attribute much of this problem to the increasing medicalization of childbirth—the growing reliance on medical interventions even in pregnancies considered low risk. Although advances in obstetric medicine have significantly improved maternal and infant outcomes, unnecessary interventions such as cesarean sections, oxytocin administration, and episiotomies have become increasingly common. Italy now has one of the highest cesarean section rates in Europe and continues to report widespread use of these procedures (Spina, 2023).

From a bioethical perspective, obstetric paternalism emerges when a physician’s commitment to beneficence conflicts with a patient’s right to make informed decisions about her own medical care. Numerous Italian mothers have reported inadequate informed consent, coercion, and pressure from healthcare professionals during labor, raising serious ethical and legal concerns. Battisti’s experience illustrates how the tension between medical authority and patient autonomy can transform childbirth from a deeply personal experience into one marked by loss of control, distrust, and lasting psychological harm.

Reflection

I chose this case study because I rarely think about the process of childbirth, much less the ethical dilemmas that can arise during it. Obstetric paternalism is particularly complex because it involves balancing the interests of three primary stakeholders: the physician, the mother, and the unborn child. Healthcare professionals must make difficult decisions that consider the mother’s autonomy while also protecting the health of both the mother and her baby.

Before studying this topic, I understood that childbirth has become increasingly medicalized, but I had not fully considered the ethical consequences of relying too heavily on medical intervention. Although scientific and technological advances have undoubtedly saved countless lives, these benefits should not come at the expense of a mother’s autonomy. Medical professionals have an ethical obligation to inform patients, respect their decisions, and involve them in their own care whenever possible.

Ultimately, I believe respecting a mother’s wishes during childbirth is an ethical requirement because she is a moral agent who deserves to be treated as an end in herself. When healthcare providers impose medical decisions against her will, they risk reducing her to a means of achieving other objectives, such as minimizing legal liability or maintaining procedural efficiency. From a deontological perspective, these actions are morally impermissible because they violate the fundamental duty to respect patient autonomy.

While I recognize that most physicians genuinely act with the intention of beneficence, the increasing medicalization of childbirth has contributed to the overuse of certain procedures that can negatively affect a mother’s physical and psychological well-being. Battisti’s case demonstrates that pursuing favorable medical outcomes should never justify disregarding informed consent or a patient’s right to participate in decisions regarding her own body.

The Ethical Dilemma

One of the primary ethical concerns in Battisti’s case is the apparent lack of informed consent. To avoid paternalism, healthcare professionals must clearly explain the purpose, risks, benefits, and alternatives of any proposed medical intervention. Most importantly, physicians must respect a competent patient’s right to accept or refuse treatment, even when they disagree with that decision (Sciarrino, 2026b). In Battisti’s case, medical staff reportedly failed to explain why a cesarean section was necessary, did not discuss potential alternatives, and proceeded without obtaining her informed consent.

A second ethical concern is the use of coercion. Pressuring a patient into accepting a medical procedure—particularly during the physical pain and emotional vulnerability of labor—undermines voluntary decision-making and compromises patient autonomy. Battisti stated that physicians insisted her baby was in danger while dismissing her repeated requests for an explanation or for a natural birth. Under these circumstances, compliance becomes difficult to distinguish from genuine consent because the patient is left with little opportunity to make an informed, voluntary decision.

From the perspective of the medical staff, their actions may have been motivated by the principle of beneficence, which emphasizes acting in a patient’s best interests. Cesarean sections can be life-saving procedures when medically necessary and often provide physicians with a controlled and predictable method of delivery that may reduce complications and legal liability. From a utilitarian perspective, proponents could argue that increased medical intervention maximizes overall well-being by improving outcomes for mothers and infants, even if doing so occasionally requires overriding a patient’s preferences.

The ethical dilemma therefore centers on two competing moral obligations: respecting a mother’s autonomy while protecting the health and safety of both mother and child. Determining when, if ever, physicians are justified in overriding a competent patient’s wishes remains one of the most challenging questions in biomedical ethics. Battisti’s case illustrates how difficult it can be to balance beneficence with respect for individual autonomy when urgent medical decisions must be made during childbirth.

Ethical Evaluation

From a deontological perspective, I believe the actions of the medical staff in Alessandra Battisti’s case were ethically unacceptable. Deontological ethics emphasizes the inherent dignity and autonomy of every individual, maintaining that patients should never be treated merely as a means to achieve a desired outcome. Regardless of the intentions behind a medical decision, physicians have a moral duty to respect a competent patient’s right to make informed choices about her own body.

Battisti’s case is an example of strong paternalism, which occurs when healthcare professionals override the expressed wishes of a competent patient who is capable of making her own decisions (Sciarrino, 2026a). Battisti repeatedly requested a natural birth and asked for an explanation of the proposed cesarean section, yet her objections were disregarded. By proceeding without her informed consent, the medical staff prioritized their own judgment over her autonomy.

Some physicians may argue that labor places women under extreme physical and emotional stress, making paternalistic intervention ethically justifiable. This reasoning aligns more closely with weak paternalism, which permits intervention when a patient’s decision-making capacity is significantly impaired (Sciarrino, 2026a). However, experiencing pain or emotional distress during childbirth does not automatically render a woman incapable of making informed decisions. A mother may be under tremendous stress while still clearly understanding her preferences and communicating them effectively. Assuming otherwise risks equating vulnerability with incompetence and undermines the ethical principle of respect for persons.

The World Health Organization has identified disrespectful, abusive, coercive, or non-consensual treatment during childbirth as a violation of women’s human rights (WHO, 2014). Likewise, the Obstetric Violence Observatory reported that approximately four out of every ten women surveyed experienced practices that violated their dignity or physical integrity during childbirth (Cicali, 2018). These findings demonstrate that obstetric paternalism extends beyond an individual ethical dispute and represents a broader public health and human rights concern.

Italy’s historical relationship with the Catholic Church further complicates these ethical questions. The concept of imago Dei, or the belief that human beings are created in the image of God, emphasizes the sanctity of human life and encourages physicians to preserve life whenever possible (Sciarrino, 2026b). While this ethical tradition may reinforce a physician’s commitment to beneficence, it does not justify violating a competent patient’s autonomy or disregarding informed consent. Protecting maternal and fetal health should remain a central goal of obstetric care, but it must be pursued alongside—not at the expense of—a woman’s fundamental right to make decisions regarding her own body.

Ultimately, I believe Alessandra Battisti’s experience illustrates why patient autonomy must remain a cornerstone of ethical medical practice. Respecting informed consent not only preserves individual dignity but also strengthens trust between patients and healthcare providers. Childbirth should be guided by collaboration rather than coercion, ensuring that mothers receive compassionate, evidence-based care while maintaining the right to actively participate in decisions about their own treatment.
