The Renormalization of Smoking Among Generation Z

Ryan Trudeau

Public Health
University of Utah

Public Health July 2026 12 min read

Abstract

Decades of public health campaigns succeeded in dramatically reducing cigarette use across the United States, yet Generation Z is now experiencing a troubling reversal of those gains. While traditional cigarette smoking has declined overall, new nicotine delivery systems, shifting cultural attitudes, and the pervasive influence of social media have contributed to a renormalization of smoking behaviors among young people born between 1997 and 2012. This article examines the mechanisms driving this renormalization—including the aestheticization of smoking on digital platforms, targeted industry marketing, the ongoing youth mental health crisis, and the blurring of boundaries between tobacco products, electronic cigarettes, and illicit substances. It argues that existing public health frameworks have not adequately adapted to the digital media environment that shapes Generation Z’s perceptions of risk and social identity. Addressing this trend requires updated prevention strategies that meet young people where they spend the majority of their time and that account for the complex social and psychological factors underlying renewed tobacco use.

Keywords

Smoking, Generation Z, Tobacco, Nicotine, Public Health, Social Media, Youth Health, Vaping, Health Policy, Substance Use

Introduction

For much of the twentieth century, smoking was embedded in American culture as a symbol of sophistication, rebellion, and social belonging. The tobacco industry spent billions of dollars constructing that image, and by 1965 more than 42% of American adults smoked cigarettes (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC], 2023). Decades of sustained public health effort—including the landmark 1964 Surgeon General’s Report, the Master Settlement Agreement, comprehensive indoor smoking bans, graphic warning labels, and youth-targeted prevention campaigns—gradually dismantled that cultural norm. By the early 2010s, adult smoking rates had fallen below 20%, and cigarette use among high school students had reached historic lows (CDC, 2023).

Yet the story did not end there. Generation Z, the cohort born roughly between 1997 and 2012, came of age in the aftermath of those victories. They grew up being told that smoking was dangerous and socially unacceptable. Despite this, new evidence suggests that smoking behaviors—broadly defined to include cigarettes, electronic nicotine delivery systems, and other tobacco products—are experiencing a cultural rehabilitation among this generation. The mechanisms of this renormalization are not the television advertisements of the 1950s but rather the algorithm-curated feeds of TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube, where smoking appears alongside carefully constructed aesthetic identities that carry significant social currency among young people.

Understanding why smoking is being renormalized among Generation Z is an urgent public health challenge. This article examines the historical context of anti-tobacco progress, the specific pathways through which smoking has regained cultural legitimacy among young people, and the public health implications of these trends. It also considers alternative perspectives and concludes with recommendations for updated prevention approaches suited to the digital era.

The Arc of Anti-Tobacco Progress

The modern tobacco control movement is widely regarded as one of public health’s greatest achievements. Following the 1964 Surgeon General’s Report, which conclusively linked cigarette smoking to lung cancer and other serious diseases, the United States embarked on a multi-decade effort to reduce tobacco use. This effort included mandatory warning labels on cigarette packaging beginning in 1966, the prohibition of broadcast advertising of tobacco products in 1971, and the establishment of comprehensive clean indoor air laws across most states by the early 2000s (American Lung Association, 2022).

Youth-specific prevention programs became a cornerstone of this strategy. Campaigns such as truth® and state-level initiatives emphasized the manipulative practices of the tobacco industry and the deceptive nature of cigarette marketing. These approaches proved especially effective with adolescents because they reframed smoking not as a rebellious act of independence but as submission to corporate manipulation. By 2019, just 5.8% of high school students reported current cigarette smoking, compared to 28% in 1991 (CDC, 2020).

The policy and educational landscape that Generation Z inherited was therefore one in which the harms of smoking had been extensively documented, and smoking had largely been pushed to the margins of social acceptability. It would be reasonable to expect this generation to continue the downward trend. Instead, a confluence of new products, new platforms, and new social pressures has created conditions for a partial but meaningful reversal.

Social Media and the Aestheticization of Smoking

One of the most significant drivers of smoking’s renormalization among Generation Z is its prominent and often glamorized presence on social media platforms. Unlike the straightforward product advertising of previous decades, contemporary smoking imagery on digital platforms is embedded within broader aesthetic frameworks that carry substantial social appeal. Researchers have documented a phenomenon sometimes referred to as “smoking aesthetics” on platforms such as Tumblr, Instagram, and TikTok, in which cigarettes and smoking behaviors appear as stylized visual elements associated with artistic identity, romantic intrigue, and countercultural sensibility (Allem et al., 2017).

On TikTok alone, content tagged with smoking-related hashtags has accumulated billions of views, frequently featuring cigarettes as props within broader lifestyle narratives rather than as explicit product endorsements. A study by Vassey et al. (2022) found that TikTok content depicting tobacco use was frequently presented in a positive or neutral light and that such content was widely available to users under the age of eighteen despite platform age restrictions. Critically, this content is rarely produced by tobacco companies directly; instead, it is created by ordinary users for whom smoking serves as a component of a carefully curated personal brand.

This bottom-up aestheticization is in many ways more difficult to counter than traditional advertising. Anti-tobacco campaigns have historically been designed to challenge corporate messaging, but peer-generated content operates through mechanisms of social identification and aspirational modeling that are harder to disrupt with warning labels or educational interventions. When a young person’s favorite creator smokes a cigarette, that act is encoded not as corporate manipulation but as authentic self-expression.

The introduction and rapid proliferation of electronic cigarettes has compounded this dynamic. Vaping devices, which entered the mass market in the mid-2000s and achieved widespread youth adoption by the mid-2010s, presented a visually distinct and technologically novel form of nicotine consumption that was initially perceived as fundamentally different from traditional smoking. Companies such as JUUL Labs designed products that were sleek, discreet, and available in flavors explicitly appealing to younger consumers. By 2018, 20.8% of high school students reported current e-cigarette use—a 78% increase from the prior year—making it the most commonly used tobacco product among youth (CDC, 2020).

Vaping’s normalization has had downstream effects on traditional cigarette use. Research by Watkins et al. (2021) found that adolescents who initiated nicotine use through e-cigarettes were significantly more likely to subsequently adopt cigarette smoking than their non-vaping peers, suggesting that vaping serves as a gateway to broader tobacco use rather than a contained, self-limiting behavior. The blurring of distinctions between vaping and smoking has also made it more difficult for young people to apply anti-smoking norms to their own behavior; if vaping is not “really” smoking, the decades of cultural messaging around cigarettes feel less relevant.

The Youth Mental Health Crisis and Smoking as Coping

Generation Z has been identified as the most psychologically distressed generation in recent recorded history. Rates of anxiety, depression, and suicidal ideation among adolescents and young adults increased sharply throughout the 2010s and were further accelerated by the COVID-19 pandemic (Twenge et al., 2022). The American Psychological Association reported in 2022 that 46% of adults aged 18–34 reported a clinically significant increase in stress levels over the preceding five years, with young women reporting especially high rates of anxiety and depressive symptoms.

There is a well-established bidirectional relationship between mental health and tobacco use. Individuals experiencing depression, anxiety, and chronic stress are substantially more likely to smoke, and nicotine use, while providing short-term relief through its effects on dopamine and stress-response systems, exacerbates underlying mental health conditions over time (Ziedonis et al., 2008). For Generation Z, the mental health crisis has therefore created a large population of young people who are both psychologically vulnerable and socially primed—through media exposure—to view smoking as a plausible coping mechanism.

Focus group research with college-aged smokers has found that smoking is frequently framed in terms of stress management and emotional self-regulation. Participants describe cigarette breaks as structured moments of pause and relief in environments characterized by academic pressure, social anxiety, and economic uncertainty (Villanti et al., 2019). This framing positions smoking as a form of self-care rather than self-harm, making it especially resistant to messaging strategies that emphasize long-term health consequences over immediate emotional benefits.

The relationship between mental health and smoking also operates through social networks. Students who smoke report that cigarette breaks provide structured social opportunities that feel less socially demanding than other forms of interaction, an appealing feature for a generation that has been socialized increasingly through digital rather than face-to-face communication. This social function of smoking adds another layer of reinforcement that is difficult to address through information-based prevention alone.

Industry Adaptation and Targeted Marketing

The tobacco industry has not passively observed demographic shifts; it has actively worked to identify and cultivate new markets. Following the restrictions imposed by the 1998 Master Settlement Agreement, major tobacco companies shifted marketing budgets toward point-of-sale advertising, digital platforms that offer limited regulatory oversight, and the development of product lines specifically designed to appeal to younger consumers. Analysis of internal tobacco industry documents has revealed systematic strategies to appeal to Generation Z through authenticity-coded messaging that mimics the aesthetic language of youth culture rather than traditional advertising conventions (Jackler et al., 2019).

Flavored tobacco products have been a central element of this strategy. Despite the FDA’s 2009 ban on flavored cigarettes other than menthol, flavored cigars, cigarillos, hookah tobacco, and e-cigarettes remained largely unregulated for years. Research has consistently found that flavored products are disproportionately used by young people; in 2020, 82.9% of youth tobacco users reported using a flavored tobacco product (CDC, 2021). The appeal of fruit and candy flavors directly reduces the perceived distance between tobacco use and ordinary consumer behavior, lowering the psychological threshold for initiation.

Menthol cigarettes deserve particular attention in this context. Menthol has been shown to mask the harshness of cigarette smoke, facilitate deeper inhalation, and increase nicotine dependence. Black and Latinx youth are disproportionately targeted by menthol marketing and experience higher rates of menthol cigarette use than their white peers (Tobacco-Related Disease Research Program, 2021). The FDA’s proposed rule to ban menthol cigarettes, announced in 2022, has faced sustained legal and political opposition, demonstrating the continued power of the tobacco industry to delay regulatory action that would reduce youth smoking.

Alternative View

It is important to acknowledge that not all evidence points uniformly toward a crisis-level renormalization of smoking among Generation Z. Overall cigarette smoking rates among adults aged 18–24 continue to be lower than those observed in previous generations at the same age, and longitudinal data show that daily cigarette smoking remains at historically low levels among high school students (CDC, 2023). Some researchers argue that concerns about social media’s influence on youth smoking are overstated, noting that exposure to smoking imagery online does not automatically translate into smoking behavior and that many young people who engage with smoking-related content are doing so ironically or as participants in a visual aesthetic rather than as aspiring smokers.

There is also reason to believe that Generation Z is more health-conscious in certain respects than prior generations. Survey data indicate that young people are aware of the risks associated with smoking and that health concerns remain among the most frequently cited reasons for not initiating or for quitting tobacco use (Villanti et al., 2019). Prevention programs that emphasize the manipulative practices of the tobacco industry continue to resonate with this demographically skeptical cohort.

However, these more optimistic data points should not obscure the genuine concern embedded in current trends. The relevant question is not whether traditional cigarette smoking is at its historical nadir but whether the broader landscape of nicotine and tobacco use—encompassing e-cigarettes, heated tobacco products, nicotine pouches, hookah, and cigars—is becoming more or less normalized among young people. On this broader measure, the evidence for renormalization is considerably more robust. The decline of combustible cigarettes has not been matched by equivalent declines in nicotine use overall; rather, alternative products have largely replaced cigarettes while preserving and in some cases intensifying nicotine dependence among young users.

Conclusion

The hard-won public health gains of the anti-tobacco movement are under pressure from a new generation of products, a transformed media environment, and a profound youth mental health crisis that makes nicotine use a tempting coping strategy for millions of young Americans. Generation Z did not live through the deaths of grandparents and parents from smoking-related diseases in the way that earlier cohorts did; they grew up in a world where tobacco’s dangers were historical fact rather than personal experience. Combined with the novelty of vaping, the pervasiveness of smoking imagery on social media, and the ongoing influence of sophisticated industry marketing, this generational distance from tobacco’s worst consequences has created conditions ripe for renormalization.

Reversing this trend will require public health approaches that go beyond the information-deficit model underlying much traditional anti-tobacco education. Young people are not unaware of the health risks; they are operating within social, emotional, and media environments that systematically undermine the salience of those risks. Effective interventions must engage with social media platforms directly, demanding greater accountability for tobacco-related content and investing in counter-messaging that speaks to the values and concerns of Generation Z. They must also address the mental health crisis that makes nicotine use emotionally appealing, integrating tobacco prevention into the broader framework of youth mental health support.

Regulatory action remains essential. Strengthening enforcement of age verification requirements for online tobacco sales, expanding flavor bans to cover all tobacco product categories, and establishing clear guidelines for tobacco content on digital platforms are all necessary steps. At the same time, these structural interventions must be accompanied by community-level programs that address the social contexts in which young people smoke—not just the products they use.

The story of tobacco control is one of remarkable public health progress, but it is an ongoing story, not a completed one. Generation Z’s relationship with smoking and nicotine is not yet fully written, and the choices made now by public health professionals, educators, regulators, and platform companies will play a significant role in determining whether this generation continues the long arc toward a tobacco-free society or whether the renormalization of smoking becomes a permanent feature of their generational identity.
