Forex Exchange Markets: The Euro-Dollar Relationship in the Post-COVID Era

Miller Smith

Finance & Marketing student at the University of Washington Foster School of Business

Global & International Affairs July 2026 15 min read

Abstract

The post-COVID era has placed renewed pressure on the international monetary system, particularly the relationship between the U.S. dollar and the euro. This article examines how pandemic-era fiscal policy, monetary divergence, the Russia-Ukraine war, sanctions, energy shocks, and de-dollarization efforts have influenced the EUR/USD exchange rate and the broader reserve currency hierarchy. Although the euro remains the dollar’s strongest competitor, structural constraints—including fragmented fiscal governance and the absence of a unified safe-asset market—continue to limit its ability to challenge U.S. monetary dominance. Meanwhile, the dollar maintains its global position through deep financial markets, institutional credibility, and its central role in international trade and investment. The analysis concludes that although the global monetary system is gradually becoming more diversified, the U.S. dollar is likely to remain the world’s primary reserve currency for the foreseeable future.

Keywords

U.S. dollar, euro, reserve currency, foreign exchange, EUR/USD, BRICS, de-dollarization, international finance

Introduction

For more than seventy years, the United States dollar has served as the cornerstone of the international monetary system. Its position as the world’s dominant reserve currency began with the Bretton Woods Agreement of 1944, which established a post-World War II monetary framework by pegging participating currencies to the U.S. dollar, itself backed by gold. Although convertibility to gold ended in 1971, the dollar retained its international dominance because of the size of the U.S. economy, the liquidity of American financial markets, and continued global confidence in U.S. institutions. Today, nearly 60 percent of the world’s officially disclosed foreign exchange reserves remain denominated in U.S. dollars, while the euro accounts for approximately one-fifth of global reserves.

Since its introduction in 1999, the euro has become the dollar’s most credible competitor. The currency quickly established itself as a major force in international trade and finance, yet it has never displaced the dollar as the world’s primary reserve currency. Structural challenges—including fragmented fiscal governance, differing economic priorities among eurozone member states, and the absence of a unified sovereign debt market comparable to U.S. Treasury securities—have limited the euro’s ability to rival the dollar’s global influence.

The post-COVID period has introduced one of the most turbulent eras in modern international finance. The pandemic, unprecedented fiscal stimulus, the Russia-Ukraine war, persistent inflation, and renewed discussions surrounding de-dollarization have collectively reshaped the global financial landscape. These developments have generated significant volatility in the EUR/USD exchange rate while reigniting debate over whether the international monetary system is gradually shifting toward a more multipolar structure.

This article argues that despite growing geopolitical fragmentation and increased efforts by several nations to reduce dependence on the U.S. dollar, the dollar continues to occupy a uniquely dominant position within the international monetary system. Although the euro has expanded its role in global finance and remains the world’s second most important reserve currency, persistent structural and institutional constraints continue to limit its ability to challenge the dollar’s supremacy. Understanding these dynamics is increasingly important for policymakers, investors, businesses, and economists navigating an evolving global economy.

Dollar and Euro as Reserve Currencies

A reserve currency is a foreign currency held in substantial quantities by central banks and governments to facilitate international trade, stabilize domestic economies, and provide financial security during periods of economic uncertainty. Beyond serving as a medium of exchange for cross-border transactions, reserve currencies enable governments to maintain liquidity during financial crises, support exchange rate stability, and strengthen confidence in the international financial system.

The United States dollar has served as the world’s dominant reserve currency for more than seventy years. Although its international role originated under the Bretton Woods system, where the dollar was backed by gold at a fixed exchange rate, its continued dominance after the collapse of the gold standard in 1971 demonstrates that its strength extends far beyond convertibility to precious metals. Today, the dollar derives its position from the size of the U.S. economy, the liquidity of Treasury markets, the stability of American political and legal institutions, and widespread international confidence in U.S. financial assets. Because a significant share of global trade, investment, and sovereign borrowing is conducted in dollars, powerful network effects continue to reinforce its role as the world’s preferred reserve currency.

Reserve currency status also provides important economic advantages for the United States. Foreign governments and central banks routinely purchase U.S. Treasury securities as reserve assets, generating consistent demand for government debt even when alternative investments may offer higher returns. This sustained demand allows the U.S. government to finance borrowing at comparatively lower interest rates while supporting the dollar’s value in global financial markets (Steiner, 2016). As a result, the United States enjoys a degree of financial flexibility and borrowing capacity that few other nations possess.

When the euro was introduced in 1999, many economists viewed it as the strongest potential challenger to the dollar’s dominance. Within its first decade, the euro grew rapidly to account for nearly one-quarter of global foreign exchange reserves, prompting speculation that the international monetary system could gradually transition toward a more balanced reserve currency structure. However, the European sovereign debt crisis between 2009 and 2014 exposed significant institutional weaknesses within the eurozone, reducing investor confidence and slowing the euro’s international momentum. Although the currency has since recovered and now represents approximately one-fifth of global reserve holdings, it continues to trail the U.S. dollar by a considerable margin.

Several structural factors continue to limit the euro’s ability to rival the dollar as the world’s leading reserve currency. Unlike the United States, the eurozone does not operate under a fully unified fiscal authority or issue a single market of government debt comparable to U.S. Treasury securities. Instead, individual member states maintain separate fiscal policies and sovereign debt markets despite sharing a common currency. Differences in economic performance, public debt levels, and political priorities across the eurozone reduce the euro’s appeal as a global safe-haven asset during periods of financial uncertainty. Consequently, while the euro remains the world’s second most important reserve currency, its institutional structure continues to limit its capacity to replace the dollar as the foundation of the international monetary system.

Understanding the strengths and limitations of both currencies provides important context for evaluating the major geopolitical and economic shocks that have shaped foreign exchange markets since the COVID-19 pandemic. The following section examines how global crises—including the pandemic, the Russia-Ukraine war, and shifting geopolitical alliances—have influenced the evolving relationship between the euro and the U.S. dollar.

Shocks to the System: Post-COVID Disruptions

The years following the COVID-19 pandemic subjected the international monetary system to one of its most significant periods of instability in decades. Rather than experiencing a single economic disruption, global markets faced a series of overlapping crises, including unprecedented fiscal expansion, supply chain breakdowns, rising inflation, the Russia-Ukraine war, energy shortages, and intensifying geopolitical competition between the United States and China. Together, these events altered capital flows, exchange rate behavior, and central bank policy, creating new challenges for both the U.S. dollar and the euro.

The United States and the eurozone responded to the pandemic with aggressive—but notably different—monetary and fiscal policies. In the United States, Congress authorized approximately $5.8 trillion in fiscal stimulus, equivalent to roughly one-quarter of U.S. gross domestic product in 2020. At the same time, the Federal Reserve reduced interest rates to near zero and introduced emergency lending facilities to stabilize financial markets. These measures supported economic recovery but also contributed to elevated inflation in the years that followed.

The European Central Bank (ECB) faced a different policy environment. Entering the pandemic with interest rates already below zero, the ECB relied primarily on its Pandemic Emergency Purchase Programme (PEPP) and large-scale asset purchases to support financial conditions across the eurozone. Although these policies helped stabilize European markets, the eurozone’s fragmented fiscal structure limited the flexibility available to policymakers when compared with the United States.

These differing policy responses produced distinct outcomes in foreign exchange markets. During the early stages of the pandemic, investors sought the U.S. dollar as a safe-haven asset amid global uncertainty. As economic conditions gradually stabilized and policy expectations evolved, the dollar weakened through portions of 2021, while the euro remained comparatively stable but struggled to achieve sustained appreciation because of the eurozone’s underlying structural constraints.

Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 introduced another major shock to the international monetary system, particularly for Europe. The conflict triggered severe disruptions in global energy markets, exposed the eurozone’s dependence on Russian natural gas, and intensified inflationary pressures across the region. As energy prices surged to historic levels, European governments were forced to accelerate efforts to diversify energy sources through initiatives such as the European Union’s REPowerEU plan. Higher energy costs, supply chain disruptions, and rising food prices combined to weaken economic growth and increase uncertainty throughout the eurozone.

These developments had important consequences for the euro. Investors increasingly viewed the currency as being more vulnerable to regional geopolitical risks than the U.S. dollar. The EUR/USD exchange rate briefly reached parity in 2022, reflecting concerns over Europe’s economic outlook and reinforcing the dollar’s role as the world’s preferred safe-haven currency during periods of international instability. While the euro remained an important reserve currency, the conflict highlighted the structural challenges associated with its dependence on a politically and economically diverse monetary union.

At the same time, geopolitical competition between the United States and China accelerated discussions surrounding de-dollarization. Western sanctions imposed on Russia—including freezing nearly $300 billion in foreign reserves and restricting access to the SWIFT international payment system—demonstrated the strategic influence of the dollar within the global financial system. These actions also encouraged several countries to reconsider their reliance on dollar-based financial infrastructure.

China has responded by expanding bilateral currency swap agreements, promoting international use of the renminbi, and developing the Cross-Border Interbank Payment System (CIPS) as an alternative payment network. Despite these initiatives, the renminbi continues to represent only a small share of global foreign exchange reserves. Limited capital market openness, capital controls, and lower international confidence continue to constrain its ability to compete directly with the U.S. dollar. While alternative payment systems are gradually expanding, the dollar remains deeply embedded in global trade, finance, and reserve management.

De-Dollarization and Europe’s Push for Autonomy

Growing geopolitical tensions have intensified international discussions surrounding de-dollarization and monetary independence. Although the U.S. dollar remains the world’s dominant reserve currency, an increasing number of governments have explored ways to reduce their dependence on dollar-denominated trade, investment, and financial infrastructure. These initiatives have been motivated by concerns over economic sovereignty, exposure to financial sanctions, and the concentration of global payment systems within the U.S.-led financial order.

Among the most prominent advocates of de-dollarization is the BRICS coalition. Originally consisting of Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa, the organization has expanded to include additional member states such as Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, and Indonesia. Collectively, these countries have pursued greater use of local currencies in bilateral trade, expanded currency swap agreements, and invested in alternative payment systems intended to reduce reliance on the U.S. dollar. While these efforts vary considerably among member states, they share a common objective: increasing financial independence within an increasingly multipolar global economy.

China has emerged as the driving force behind many of these initiatives. In recent years, Beijing has promoted broader international use of the renminbi, expanded bilateral currency settlement agreements, and invested heavily in the Cross-Border Interbank Payment System (CIPS) as an alternative to the SWIFT financial messaging network. Trade between China and Russia has also shifted increasingly toward settlement in domestic currencies following Western sanctions imposed after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. Despite these developments, the renminbi continues to account for only a small share of global foreign exchange reserves. Capital controls, relatively limited financial market openness, and lower levels of international investor confidence continue to restrict its ability to compete directly with the U.S. dollar as a global reserve currency.

While BRICS nations have focused primarily on reducing dependence on the dollar, the European Union has pursued a different strategy. Rather than attempting to replace the dollar outright, European policymakers have concentrated on strengthening the euro’s international role and improving the resilience of Europe’s financial system. The European Central Bank has supported initiatives such as the proposed digital euro while encouraging deeper European capital markets and greater financial integration among member states. These reforms are intended to strengthen the euro’s position in international trade, investment, and cross-border payments while reducing Europe’s dependence on external financial infrastructure.

Despite these ambitions, significant structural obstacles remain. Unlike the United States, the eurozone lacks a unified fiscal authority and a single, highly liquid government bond market comparable to U.S. Treasury securities. Individual member states continue to maintain separate fiscal policies, debt markets, and political priorities despite sharing a common currency. These institutional differences reduce investor confidence during periods of financial uncertainty and limit the euro’s attractiveness as a global safe-haven asset.

Consequently, neither BRICS-led de-dollarization initiatives nor European monetary reforms have yet produced a realistic alternative to the existing dollar-centered international monetary system. Instead, current trends suggest that the global financial system is gradually becoming more diversified rather than fundamentally replacing the dollar. Regional payment systems, local-currency trade, and alternative financial institutions are likely to continue expanding, but the depth, liquidity, institutional stability, and global trust associated with U.S. financial markets remain difficult to replicate.

For the foreseeable future, the international monetary system is likely to become increasingly multipolar while continuing to rely on the U.S. dollar as its primary reserve currency. The euro, the renminbi, and other currencies may gradually expand their regional and international influence, but meaningful changes to the global reserve currency hierarchy are expected to occur gradually rather than through a sudden transition.

Current Exchange Rate Dynamics

The EUR/USD exchange rate has experienced considerable volatility since the COVID-19 pandemic, reflecting the combined influence of monetary policy, inflation, economic growth, and geopolitical uncertainty. While short-term currency movements are often driven by investor sentiment and market expectations, longer-term exchange rate trends generally reflect the relative economic strength and policy decisions of the United States and the eurozone. Understanding these factors is essential because fluctuations in the EUR/USD exchange rate influence international trade, investment, and financial markets around the world.

Among the most significant drivers of post-pandemic exchange rate movements has been inflation. Both the United States and the eurozone experienced their highest inflation rates in decades following the pandemic, although the causes and severity differed across regions. Within the eurozone, inflation varied considerably among member states, with some countries experiencing inflation rates exceeding 20 percent while others faced far more moderate price increases. These differences complicated policymaking for the European Central Bank (ECB), which was required to implement a single monetary policy across economies experiencing very different economic conditions.

The United States experienced a similarly challenging inflationary environment. Consumer price inflation reached a peak of 9.1 percent in June 2022, prompting the Federal Reserve to implement one of the most aggressive monetary tightening cycles in recent history. Interest rates increased from near zero to more than 5 percent in less than two years. Higher interest rates increased returns on dollar-denominated assets, attracting foreign investment and strengthening demand for the U.S. dollar. As investors shifted capital toward American financial markets, the dollar appreciated against many major currencies, including the euro.

The European Central Bank also responded by raising interest rates, but it proceeded more cautiously because of weaker economic growth and concerns about financial fragmentation within the eurozone. Higher borrowing costs affected member states differently, making rapid policy tightening more difficult than in the United States. As a result, interest rate differentials generally favored the dollar throughout much of 2022 and 2023, contributing to sustained U.S. dollar strength and placing additional downward pressure on the euro.

By 2024 and 2025, inflation had begun to moderate across both economies, allowing central banks to gradually shift toward less restrictive monetary policies. As expectations surrounding future interest rates became more closely aligned, the euro recovered a portion of its earlier losses against the dollar. At the same time, improving economic conditions within Europe, evolving U.S. trade policy, and changing investor expectations further supported renewed appreciation of the euro during 2025.

Although interest rates and inflation remain among the most important determinants of exchange rate movements, they represent only part of a much broader economic picture. Investor confidence, geopolitical developments, fiscal policy, energy markets, and expectations regarding future economic growth all influence currency valuations. Consequently, businesses engaged in international trade and investors managing globally diversified portfolios must evaluate exchange rate risk alongside more traditional financial considerations.

Forecasting exchange rates remains inherently uncertain because currency markets respond continuously to changing economic and political conditions. Nevertheless, most economists expect the U.S. dollar and the euro to remain the world’s two most influential currencies throughout the coming decade. Future movements in the EUR/USD exchange rate will likely depend on relative economic growth, inflation trends, central bank policy decisions, geopolitical developments, and the continued evolution of the international monetary system.

Viewed collectively, these factors demonstrate that currency values are not determined by a single economic indicator but by the interaction of monetary policy, market confidence, institutional stability, and global geopolitical events. The resilience of both the dollar and the euro throughout the post-pandemic period underscores their continued importance within an increasingly interconnected global economy.

Conclusion

The post-COVID era has tested the resilience of the international monetary system in ways not seen for decades. The COVID-19 pandemic, the Russia-Ukraine war, persistent inflation, shifting central bank policies, and intensifying geopolitical competition have collectively challenged long-standing assumptions about global finance. These events have fueled renewed debate over whether the international monetary system is entering a new era in which the dominance of the U.S. dollar may gradually diminish. However, the evidence examined throughout this article suggests that, despite these unprecedented disruptions, the dollar continues to occupy a uniquely dominant position within the global financial system.

Although the euro remains the world’s second most important reserve currency, it continues to face institutional and structural constraints that limit its ability to rival the dollar. Fragmented fiscal governance, differing economic conditions across eurozone member states, and the absence of a unified sovereign debt market comparable to U.S. Treasury securities reduce the euro’s attractiveness as a global safe-haven asset during periods of financial uncertainty. Nevertheless, initiatives such as the proposed digital euro and continued efforts toward deeper European financial integration demonstrate that the euro’s international role is likely to expand gradually in the years ahead.

Similarly, de-dollarization initiatives pursued by BRICS nations illustrate a broader movement toward a more diversified international monetary system. Expanded use of local currencies, regional payment systems, and alternative financial institutions reflects growing interest in reducing dependence on the U.S. dollar. Despite this momentum, no existing currency or financial system currently possesses the depth, liquidity, institutional stability, or global confidence necessary to replace the dollar as the foundation of international finance.

For investors, policymakers, and multinational businesses, understanding the evolving relationship between the dollar and the euro will remain increasingly important. Exchange rates are influenced not only by inflation and interest rates but also by geopolitical developments, fiscal policy, investor confidence, and the credibility of national institutions. As economic and political conditions continue to evolve, successfully navigating international financial markets will require careful consideration of both traditional economic indicators and broader global trends.

Ultimately, the international monetary system appears to be moving toward greater diversification rather than a complete transformation. While the influence of the euro, the renminbi, and other regional currencies is likely to continue expanding, the transition is expected to occur gradually rather than through a sudden shift in global reserve currency leadership. For the foreseeable future, the U.S. dollar is likely to remain the cornerstone of international finance, while the euro continues to strengthen its position as the world’s leading alternative reserve currency within an increasingly interconnected and multipolar global economy.
