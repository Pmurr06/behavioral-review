Growing Without Losing Your Culture: Organizational Lessons from the Bouldering Project

David Kim

Logistics, Materials & Supply Chain Management student at the University of Washington Foster School of Business

Economics & Business June 2026 10 min read

Keywords

Organizational Behavior, Organizational Culture, Leadership, Employee Engagement, Bouldering Project, Management, Workplace Culture, Organizational Strategy

Introduction

As organizations expand, growth often creates challenges that extend beyond finances or operations. Companies that begin as small, community-oriented businesses frequently struggle to preserve the culture that originally contributed to their success. Maintaining employee engagement, strengthening leadership, and protecting organizational identity become increasingly difficult as businesses introduce additional management layers, standardize operations, and expand into new markets.

The Bouldering Project illustrates this challenge. Founded in Seattle in 2011, the company has grown from a single climbing gym into a national organization with locations across the United States. While its growth has expanded access to climbing and movement-based fitness, it has also introduced new organizational pressures that influence employee experiences, workplace culture, and management practices. As the company continues scaling, balancing operational efficiency with its original community-focused identity has become an increasingly important leadership challenge.

This article examines how organizational growth has affected the Seattle Bouldering Project by analyzing employee interviews conducted across multiple Seattle-area locations. Rather than evaluating financial performance, the focus is placed on organizational behavior, including communication, leadership, motivation, workplace culture, and employee commitment. Interviews with frontline employees and managers revealed several recurring themes involving cultural disconnect, weakening manager-employee relationships, operational inefficiencies, and employee turnover.

Using established organizational behavior frameworks, this case study evaluates both the strengths and weaknesses of the company's current organizational structure. Although employees consistently described the workplace as collaborative and mission-driven, the findings suggest that continued growth may unintentionally weaken many of the cultural characteristics that originally differentiated the organization. The analysis concludes with practical recommendations designed to strengthen leadership engagement, improve employee commitment, and preserve the community-centered culture that has long defined the Bouldering Project.

Ultimately, the challenges facing the Bouldering Project are not unique. Many rapidly growing organizations must determine how to expand without sacrificing the values and relationships that fueled their early success. For businesses seeking long-term sustainability, preserving organizational culture may prove just as important as increasing operational scale.

Company Background

The Bouldering Project was founded in Seattle in 2011 by Andy Wyatt and Chris Potts with the goal of creating more than a traditional climbing gym. From its inception, the organization sought to build an inclusive community where climbing, movement, and social connection existed alongside one another. Rather than simply providing climbing walls, the company positioned itself as a space that encourages wellness, exploration, and meaningful relationships among members of all experience levels.

Over the past decade, that vision has expanded considerably. What began as a single renovated warehouse in Seattle has developed into a national organization operating multiple facilities across major metropolitan areas throughout the United States. Alongside climbing walls, locations now offer yoga classes, fitness facilities, coworking spaces, and community programming intended to create welcoming environments beyond the sport itself. This expansion has allowed the company to introduce climbing to new communities while maintaining a reputation for innovation within the indoor recreation industry.

Despite this growth, expansion inevitably introduces organizational complexity. Larger organizations require additional management layers, standardized operating procedures, and greater consistency across locations. While these changes improve operational efficiency, they can also reduce the flexibility and local identity that often characterize smaller businesses. Organizations frequently face the challenge of balancing consistency with authenticity as they transition from entrepreneurial ventures into mature businesses.

To better understand how these changes have affected employees, interviews were conducted with staff members from several Seattle-area Bouldering Project locations, including Fremont, Poplar, Upper Walls, and the University District. Interview participants represented both frontline employees and members of management, allowing the analysis to examine organizational issues from multiple perspectives. Questions focused on organizational structure, communication, job satisfaction, motivation, leadership, and workplace culture.

The interviews revealed that employees continue to value the company's mission and generally report positive working relationships. However, several recurring concerns emerged across locations. Employees described signs of a growing cultural disconnect between leadership and frontline staff, declining visibility of managers within the climbing community, operational challenges during busy periods, and relatively high turnover among entry-level employees. Although these concerns varied in severity between locations, their consistency suggested broader organizational patterns worthy of examination rather than isolated workplace complaints.

These findings provide an opportunity to examine how organizational behavior principles apply to a growing company. Rather than criticizing the Bouldering Project's expansion, this analysis seeks to understand how continued growth influences employee engagement, organizational commitment, and leadership effectiveness. In doing so, the case offers broader lessons for organizations attempting to scale while preserving the culture that originally contributed to their success.

Organizational Challenges

Although employees consistently described the Bouldering Project as an enjoyable place to work, interviews revealed several organizational challenges that have become more noticeable as the company has expanded. Rather than existing as isolated issues, many employees viewed these concerns as interconnected consequences of organizational growth. Four themes emerged repeatedly throughout the interviews: cultural disconnect, weakening manager-employee relationships, operational inefficiencies, and employee turnover.

The most frequently discussed concern involved a gradual shift in organizational culture. Employees acknowledged that growth has allowed the Bouldering Project to reach more communities, but many also believed the company has become increasingly corporate as it has expanded. Several interviewees explained that the organization originally distinguished itself through its strong community identity and local character. As standardized procedures and outside investment have become more prominent, some employees felt that maintaining operational consistency has occasionally taken priority over preserving the unique culture that initially attracted both employees and members.

Closely connected to this cultural shift was the perception that relationships between managers and frontline employees have weakened. Interview participants noted that managers historically participated actively in climbing alongside members and staff, reinforcing a shared identity within the organization. More recently, employees described management as becoming increasingly focused on administrative responsibilities and less visible on the climbing floor. While managers continue fulfilling essential operational duties, several employees believed this reduced day-to-day interaction has made leadership appear more distant from both employees and the climbing community itself.

Employees also identified operational challenges that affect daily workplace experiences. During peak hours, locations may process well over one thousand customer check-ins, creating significant pressure on frontline staff. Interviewees reported that scheduling inconsistencies, customer conflicts, occasional theft, and limited managerial presence during busy periods contributed to unnecessary workplace stress. Although these issues are not unique to the Bouldering Project, employees frequently connected them to the broader organizational changes occurring as the company continues to scale.

High employee turnover represented another recurring concern. Many entry-level employees indicated that they viewed their positions as temporary, expecting to remain with the organization for no more than two years. Because many staff members are students, part-time employees, or individuals balancing multiple jobs, turnover is naturally higher than in many full-time workplaces. However, interviewees also suggested that limited advancement opportunities and the routine nature of many entry-level responsibilities reduce long-term organizational commitment. While flexible scheduling and complimentary climbing memberships remain valuable incentives, employees generally viewed these benefits as encouraging short-term retention rather than long-term career development.

Importantly, these findings should not be interpreted as evidence of organizational failure. Employees overwhelmingly expressed appreciation for the company's mission, workplace environment, and climbing community. Instead, the interviews suggest that the Bouldering Project faces a challenge common to many growing organizations: preserving the relationships, leadership style, and organizational culture that contributed to its early success while adapting to the demands of a larger and more complex business.

Applying Organizational Behavior Theory

The interview findings become more meaningful when viewed through established organizational behavior frameworks. While the Bouldering Project demonstrates many organizational strengths—including high job satisfaction, effective communication, and a strong mission-driven culture—the interviews also illustrate how organizational growth can unintentionally alter employee motivation, organizational commitment, and leadership effectiveness.

One of the company's greatest strengths is its organizational structure. Employees consistently described communication as efficient and the chain of command as well-defined despite the company's continued expansion. Regional leadership, local managers, assistant managers, and operations managers create a structure that allows responsibilities to be delegated effectively while maintaining consistency across multiple locations. Employees generally reported little confusion regarding organizational hierarchy or decision-making, suggesting that the Bouldering Project has successfully scaled many of its operational systems without creating unnecessary bureaucracy.

Employee motivation also appears to be strongly influenced by intrinsic rather than purely financial rewards. Nearly every employee interviewed expressed a genuine passion for climbing and valued the close-knit community that surrounds the sport. This intrinsic motivation is reinforced by organizational incentives such as complimentary climbing memberships and flexible scheduling, which align closely with employee interests and lifestyles. Rather than relying solely on wages to motivate performance, the organization creates value by allowing employees to participate in the activity they enjoy while contributing to a community they identify with.

These findings also reflect principles of organizational commitment. Employees demonstrated strong affective commitment by expressing emotional attachment to both the climbing community and the organization's mission. Many interviewees described genuine loyalty toward the company, particularly because leadership historically shared their enthusiasm for climbing. Employees often respect leaders who actively participate in the organization's core mission, and several respondents indicated that management credibility is strengthened when supervisors remain engaged with the climbing community rather than functioning solely as administrators.

Stress management represents another area in which the company performs relatively well while still facing opportunities for improvement. Employees acknowledged that busy periods create demanding work environments, particularly when managing high customer volumes or coordinating numerous operational responsibilities. However, many described these challenges as motivating rather than discouraging because they are balanced by flexible scheduling, supportive coworkers, and meaningful interactions with members. Organizational behavior research often distinguishes between productive challenge stressors and harmful hindrance stressors, and many of the pressures identified by employees appear to fall into the former category when adequate managerial support is available.

Finally, the interviews highlight the importance of organizational ethics and identity. Several employees expressed concern that continued growth and increasing emphasis on profitability could gradually weaken the organization's commitment to community. While expansion is essential for long-term sustainability, organizations that lose sight of their founding mission often experience declining employee engagement and reduced organizational commitment. The Bouldering Project's challenge is therefore not choosing between growth and culture, but ensuring that both remain aligned as the company continues expanding. Successfully balancing these priorities will likely determine whether the organization can preserve the community-centered identity that has distinguished it since its founding.

Recommendations for Leadership

The interview findings suggest that the Bouldering Project possesses a strong organizational foundation but faces several leadership challenges that commonly emerge during periods of rapid growth. Rather than requiring sweeping organizational restructuring, the company would likely benefit most from reinforcing the cultural strengths that originally contributed to its success while adapting management practices to meet the demands of a larger organization.

The first recommendation is to strengthen manager-employee relationships through more visible and engaged leadership. Employees consistently emphasized the value of managers who actively participate in the climbing community rather than remaining focused exclusively on administrative responsibilities. By spending more time interacting with staff and members on the climbing floor, managers can reinforce organizational values, improve communication, and rebuild the shared identity that employees associated with the company's early culture. Leadership visibility serves not only operational purposes but also symbolic ones, demonstrating that management remains invested in the same community that employees and members value.

Regular mentorship and structured employee check-ins would further strengthen organizational commitment. Rather than limiting communication to performance evaluations or operational concerns, monthly conversations focused on employee development, workplace experiences, and professional goals could improve trust while providing managers with valuable insight into emerging organizational issues. These discussions need not become formal performance reviews; instead, they should reinforce open communication and demonstrate that employee perspectives remain important as the organization continues expanding.

The company should also continue investing in employee development through practical training and collaborative learning opportunities. Standard operating procedures can improve consistency while reducing unnecessary dependence on managerial intervention during routine tasks. At the same time, team-based activities—such as collaborative route-setting projects or staff climbing events—can strengthen interpersonal relationships while reinforcing the organization's community-centered culture. These activities encourage employees to develop professionally while maintaining the sense of shared purpose that originally distinguished the Bouldering Project from more traditional fitness facilities.

Finally, the organization should carefully evaluate how pricing decisions influence accessibility and community identity. Interview participants expressed concern that rising membership costs may unintentionally limit participation to individuals with greater financial resources, potentially weakening the inclusive environment the company seeks to promote. While pricing decisions must remain financially sustainable, leadership should continue exploring strategies that balance profitability with accessibility. Expanding promotional programs, introductory memberships, or community partnerships may help broaden participation without requiring substantial reductions in revenue.

Taken together, these recommendations do not require the Bouldering Project to abandon its current growth strategy. Instead, they encourage leadership to view organizational culture as a strategic asset deserving the same attention as operational efficiency. Companies often succeed because of the relationships they cultivate as much as the services they provide. Preserving those relationships while continuing to grow will likely remain one of the organization's greatest leadership challenges—and one of its greatest competitive advantages.

Conclusion

The Bouldering Project demonstrates that organizational growth brings both opportunity and complexity. Expanding from a single climbing gym into a nationally recognized organization has allowed the company to introduce climbing, wellness, and community-building to thousands of new members. At the same time, continued expansion has created new challenges surrounding leadership visibility, employee engagement, organizational culture, and operational consistency. As with many growing organizations, maintaining the qualities that originally defined the company becomes increasingly difficult as additional management layers and standardized procedures are introduced.

Despite these challenges, employee interviews revealed that the organization possesses a remarkably strong foundation. Employees consistently reported high levels of job satisfaction, meaningful relationships with coworkers, and a genuine passion for the climbing community. Communication within the organization was generally viewed as effective, leadership was respected, and the company's mission remained a significant source of employee motivation. These strengths provide the organization with an excellent platform from which to address the issues identified throughout this analysis.

The findings suggest that preserving organizational culture should become a strategic priority alongside continued operational growth. Strengthening manager-employee relationships, increasing leadership visibility within the climbing community, expanding mentorship opportunities, and maintaining accessibility all reinforce the values that helped establish the Bouldering Project's reputation. Rather than viewing organizational culture as separate from business performance, leadership should recognize that employee engagement, community trust, and organizational identity contribute directly to long-term success.

Although this case study focuses specifically on the Bouldering Project, its lessons extend far beyond the climbing industry. Many organizations face similar challenges as they transition from entrepreneurial ventures into larger enterprises. The ability to scale operations without sacrificing employee commitment, authentic leadership, or community identity remains one of the defining challenges of organizational growth. Businesses that successfully balance these competing priorities are often those that sustain both financial performance and organizational culture over the long term.
