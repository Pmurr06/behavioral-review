Hidden Costs of Global Seafood: Child and Migrant Labor in Thailand’s Shrimp Industry

Ella Roehl

Human Physiology student at the University of Iowa

Global & International Affairs July 2026 8 min read

Introduction

Thailand has long been one of the world’s leading exporters of shrimp, supplying seafood to consumers across North America, Europe, and Asia. As global demand for shrimp has increased, Thailand’s seafood industry has expanded rapidly to meet international market needs. While this growth has generated significant economic benefits, it has also drawn attention to serious labor concerns within portions of the industry’s supply chain. Reports from international organizations, journalists, and human rights advocates have documented the exploitation of vulnerable migrant workers and the use of child labor in some seafood processing facilities.

Many of these workers, particularly migrants from neighboring Myanmar, seek employment in Thailand because of limited economic opportunities in their home country. However, their vulnerable legal and financial circumstances often place them at greater risk of exploitation, unsafe working conditions, and wage abuse. These concerns have become an important issue in discussions surrounding ethical sourcing, globalization, and labor rights.

This article examines how rising global demand for shrimp has contributed to labor challenges within Thailand’s seafood industry. By exploring the relationship between international consumer demand, migrant labor, and workplace conditions, it highlights the broader ethical questions surrounding global supply chains and the responsibility of governments, corporations, and consumers in protecting workers’ rights.

Rising Global Demand and the Expansion of Thailand’s Shrimp Industry

Global demand for shrimp has grown substantially over the past several decades as seafood has become an increasingly popular source of protein. Consumers are often drawn to shrimp because of its versatility, nutritional value, and relatively low fat content, making it a common component of diets across many regions of the world. Market analyses project continued growth in the global shrimp industry, driven by rising incomes, expanding international trade, and increased consumer interest in protein-rich foods (Future Market Insights, 2023).

Thailand has emerged as one of the world’s leading shrimp exporters, supplying seafood to major international markets including the United States, Europe, and Japan. As demand has increased, producers have faced growing pressure to maintain high production levels while remaining competitive in the global marketplace. This competitive environment has encouraged many companies to prioritize efficiency and cost reduction throughout the seafood supply chain.

Maintaining low production costs often depends on access to a large and affordable labor force. According to research conducted by the International Labour Organization (ILO) and The Asia Foundation, Thailand’s seafood industry employs approximately 700,000 workers, with migrant laborers accounting for nearly 80 percent of the workforce. Many of these workers migrate from neighboring Myanmar in search of economic opportunity and are employed in seafood processing, packaging, and related industries (The Asia Foundation & International Labour Organization, 2015).

While migrant labor plays an essential role in supporting Thailand’s seafood exports, these workers frequently occupy positions that are physically demanding, low-paying, and difficult to fill through the domestic labor market alone. Language barriers, uncertain legal status, limited labor protections, and economic vulnerability may increase the risk of exploitation for some migrant workers. These conditions demonstrate how the pressures created by global consumer demand can extend far beyond the marketplace, influencing labor practices throughout international supply chains.

Migrant Labor and Exploitation Within the Seafood Supply Chain

The rapid expansion of Thailand’s shrimp industry has increased reliance on migrant workers, particularly individuals from neighboring Myanmar seeking employment opportunities. Although migrant labor has become essential to the industry’s success, numerous investigations have documented labor practices that expose some workers to unsafe conditions, excessive working hours, and limited legal protections. These issues have raised concerns among international organizations and human rights advocates regarding labor standards within portions of Thailand’s seafood supply chain.

A joint report by The Asia Foundation and the International Labour Organization (ILO) found that migrant workers make up a significant majority of Thailand’s seafood processing workforce. While many migrants migrate voluntarily in search of higher wages, language barriers, uncertain immigration status, debt, and limited access to legal protections can leave workers particularly vulnerable to exploitation. These vulnerabilities may discourage employees from reporting unsafe working conditions or labor violations for fear of losing their employment or facing deportation (The Asia Foundation & ILO, 2015).

Investigative reporting has also brought international attention to allegations of child labor and forced labor within segments of the seafood industry. A 2007 Reuters investigation cited human rights organizations that described some processing facilities as operating under conditions resembling modern-day slavery, documenting cases involving children and undocumented migrant workers performing long hours in hazardous environments (Cropley, 2007). Although these reports do not represent every seafood processing operation in Thailand, they illustrate serious labor abuses that have occurred within parts of the industry’s supply chain.

The persistence of these conditions reflects broader challenges associated with globalization and international production networks. When consumers demand inexpensive seafood and companies compete in highly competitive global markets, economic pressure to reduce production costs may increase. Without effective labor enforcement, transparent supply chains, and corporate accountability, vulnerable workers may bear the greatest burden of meeting worldwide demand. Consequently, protecting migrant workers requires not only stronger labor regulations but also continued oversight from governments, businesses, and international organizations committed to ethical sourcing and human rights.

Conclusion

Thailand’s shrimp industry demonstrates how global consumer demand can shape labor practices throughout international supply chains. As shrimp has become an increasingly popular food worldwide, the industry’s rapid expansion has created significant economic opportunities while also exposing weaknesses in labor oversight and worker protections. Reports from international organizations and investigative journalists suggest that some migrant workers and children have experienced exploitative working conditions while contributing to one of the world’s largest seafood export industries.

The evidence indicates that labor exploitation cannot be attributed solely to consumer demand. Rather, it results from a combination of factors, including intense global market competition, pressure to reduce production costs, inadequate labor enforcement, and the vulnerability of migrant workers seeking economic opportunity. Addressing these issues requires cooperation among governments, seafood producers, international organizations, and importing countries to strengthen labor protections, improve supply chain transparency, and ensure that labor standards are effectively enforced.

Consumers also play an important role in encouraging ethical production practices. Increased awareness of labor conditions has led many companies to adopt stronger sourcing standards and independent auditing programs designed to improve accountability throughout the seafood supply chain. Supporting businesses that prioritize ethical labor practices can encourage continued improvements while helping reduce the risk of exploitation.

Ultimately, Thailand’s shrimp industry illustrates both the opportunities and challenges created by globalization. International trade has generated employment and economic growth, yet it has also revealed the importance of protecting vulnerable workers whose labor supports global markets. Continued collaboration between policymakers, businesses, and consumers will be essential to promoting a seafood industry that is both economically successful and socially responsible.
