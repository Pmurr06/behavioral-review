The Body That Drives: Merleau-Ponty’s Phenomenology of Embodiment in Baby Driver

Harrison McLain

Computer Science student at Gonzaga University

Introduction

In the opening scene of Edgar Wright’s 2017 film Baby Driver, the young getaway driver Baby sits parked outside an Atlanta bank. He has his earbuds in and is waiting for the criminals he works with to finish a robbery. When they come sprinting out and pile into the car, he pulls into traffic with the ease of someone walking through a familiar room. When Baby drives, there is no visible calculation or deliberate decision-making—only movement and, beneath that movement, music. Baby drives the way an expert typist types or the way a dancer dances. The body is simply doing what it already knows, and the mind is along for the ride.

This quality of total physical absorption is not simply a character trait or cinematic embellishment; it is a philosophical position. One that Edgar Wright’s film embodies with remarkable precision. Baby Driver enacts the central claims of Maurice Merleau-Ponty’s phenomenology of embodiment. The body is not a tool the mind operates but the very subject of experience. Skilled action proceeds pre-reflectively through a body-schema that can incorporate objects and rhythms into itself. The film also suggests that trauma and habit are not merely memories stored in the mind but are layered into the body’s own structure.

Wright’s directional choices—including his synchronized editing, embodied camerawork, and treatment of diegetic music—not only illustrate these ideas but actively produce them, making Baby Driver one of the most phenomenological films in recent cinema.

I. The Problem with Descartes

To understand what Merleau-Ponty is arguing, it helps to understand what he is arguing against. In the seventeenth century, René Descartes proposed a strict separation between mind and body. He described the mind as conscious and immaterial, while the body was merely an object—a kind of biological machine that the mind inhabits and directs. This view became known as Cartesian dualism.

Cartesian dualism became so deeply embedded in Western thought that it continues to shape assumptions we rarely stop to examine. It is common to think that perception is a matter of the mind receiving sensory data from the body, that action is the mind issuing commands to the body, and that the body itself is largely passive in this process.

Maurice Merleau-Ponty’s Phenomenology of Perception (1945) argues that this picture is fundamentally mistaken. Merleau-Ponty does not deny that humans think, reflect, and reason. What he rejects is the idea that these capacities are primary or foundational. Beneath conscious thought lies a more basic mode of engagement with the world—one that is bodily.

We do not first have experiences in our minds and then use our bodies to act on them. Instead, we exist as bodies that move and engage with the world. Conscious reflection occasionally arises from this prior bodily engagement rather than governing it from above. As Merleau-Ponty writes, “the body is our general medium for having a world.” The body is not simply a vessel for consciousness; it is the form consciousness takes.

This perspective fundamentally changes how we understand skilled action, perception, memory, and even identity. It also changes how we might watch a film like Baby Driver, where the most important events are happening through a character’s body rather than through deliberate thought.

II. The Body-Schema and the Extended Body

The concept at the center of Merleau-Ponty’s account of skilled bodily action is the body-schema. This is the body’s pre-conscious, implicit map of its own position and orientation in space. When you reach for a glass without looking, you are not calculating the distance between your hand and the glass. Your body already knows, and it acts on that knowledge without waiting for your conscious mind to catch up. The body-schema is this implicit spatial self-knowledge.

What makes the body-schema especially useful for reading Baby Driver is Merleau-Ponty’s observation that it can expand to include objects and instruments. For example, when a surgeon uses a scalpel, enough practice allows the surgeon to stop feeling the scalpel in their hand and instead feel the tissue at the blade’s tip. The instrument has been incorporated into the body-schema and has become part of how the surgeon perceives the world. The same is true for a blind person’s cane. After a period of use, the cane is no longer felt as an object being held; instead, what is felt is the ground at its end. The cane has extended the body-schema outward.

This is exactly what happens with Baby and his car. The film makes this visible through the quality of his driving. He never looks at the edges of his car because they are his edges. When he executes a reverse turn or slides between two vehicles with inches of clearance, there is no visible judgment—only action. His body-schema has incorporated the car so fully that its dimensions are felt rather than computed. He is not a person operating a vehicle from inside it; he is a body whose spatial extent happens to include a vehicle.

The music complicates and deepens this picture. Baby’s tinnitus, a persistent ringing caused by a childhood car accident, means he cannot exist in silence without being reminded of the ringing in his ears. His solution is to wear earbuds constantly and play music over the ringing. As the film progresses, however, it becomes clear that the music is not merely masking the noise—it is structuring his experience of space and time. He synchronizes his actions to beats. He waits for the right bar of a song before starting a vehicle. He times turns, acceleration, and stops to musical phrases. The music has been incorporated into his body-schema just as the car has.

One way to think about this is that if the car extends Baby’s body through space, the music structures his experience through time. The music gives his bodily engagement with the world a tempo. His body-schema is not only spatial—it is rhythmic.

III. Pre-Reflective Experience and the Intentional Arc

Merleau-Ponty distinguishes between pre-reflective and reflective modes of experience. Reflective experience is conscious and sequential. You notice something, think about it, decide what to do, and then act. Pre-reflective experience is different. It is immediate and bodily, and it does not wait for deliberation.

When an experienced driver navigates a familiar road, they are not going through the sequence of perceiving each bend and consciously deciding to steer into it. The body is already steering before conscious awareness catches up. This is the normal condition of skilled bodily activity.

Related to this is what Merleau-Ponty calls the intentional arc. The intentional arc is the body’s anticipatory orientation toward its environment. The body is never simply reacting to stimuli; it is already oriented toward the situation it is in. Merleau-Ponty writes that “consciousness is in the first place not a matter of ‘I think that’ but of ‘I can.’” Not cognition but capacity, not thought but readiness.

Baby’s behavior throughout the film is defined by this pre-reflective readiness. There is a moment when Doc finishes a job briefing and the other criminals ask questions and express doubts. Baby says nothing. He does not need to deliberate because his body is already in the situation in a way that renders deliberation unnecessary. His intentional arc is engaged. He is already, at the level of the body, in the action before it begins.

The film also dramatizes the cost of disrupting this pre-reflective state. When Baby is forced into reflection by circumstances that interrupt the flow of his bodily engagement, requiring him to consciously think about what he is doing, he is visibly less certain, less fluid, and less himself. The film’s most dangerous moments are often the ones in which Baby cannot simply act but must stop and decide. Consciousness, in the Cartesian sense, is not his most fundamental mode of being in the world. His body is.

IV. Trauma, Habit, and the Sedimented Body

If the body-schema and the intentional arc describe Baby’s skilled action, Merleau-Ponty’s concept of sedimentation helps account for why Baby is the way he is. Merleau-Ponty borrows the term from geology. Just as sediment accumulates in layers that shape the structure of the earth over time, past experiences deposit themselves as layers of tendency and habit in the body. These deposits are not stored as memories the mind can consciously access. Rather, they become structural, shaping how the body is oriented toward the world.

Baby’s childhood car accident is presented as the origin of his tinnitus. The film treats tinnitus not as backstory but as a permanent phenomenological condition. The ringing does not let him forget his body. Most people, when absorbed in activity, can lose awareness of themselves as embodied. Baby cannot. The ringing is a constant reminder of bodily presence—a reminder that the body is always there.

Merleau-Ponty, in his discussions of phantom limbs and chronic pain, argues that illness and injury do not simply impair the body from the outside; they reorganize one’s entire perceptual world. The crash did not just leave Baby with a medical condition. It left him with a different world—a more bodily one, one that could only be made livable through music.

Baby cannot simply remember his past. His body needs to inscribe it in something he can play back and physically experience. This is what it looks like when memory is motor rather than intellectual.

Sedimentation also clarifies the structure of Baby’s bondage to Doc, the crime boss to whom he owes a debt. Doc’s control over Baby is not merely a legal or financial arrangement; it operates at the level of the body. Baby has been driving for Doc for years, and that history is sedimented into him. It is part of the lean of his intentional arc and part of the habit-structure of his body. He cannot simply decide to stop driving any more than a pianist can simply decide to forget the keyboard.

The debt is not only financial—it is bodily. It is this that makes the film’s climactic arc, Baby’s gradual reorientation toward a different life through his relationship with Debora, something more than a conventional redemption narrative. It is a phenomenological event, representing the slow redirection of a sedimented body.

V. Style as Phenomenological Argument

What distinguishes Baby Driver from a film that merely uses Merleau-Ponty’s ideas as a theme is the degree to which Wright’s stylistic choices enact those ideas at the level of cinematic experience. The film does not simply tell us that Baby is an embodied person; it attempts to place us in the position of one.

The film’s most discussed feature upon its release was its synchronization of edits, sound effects, and character actions to the music on Baby’s playlist. Cuts land on beats, and incidental sounds in the environment fall into rhythm with the music. This technique is on full display during the sequence in which Baby walks to a coffee shop while listening to “Harlem Shuffle.” Street sounds align with the song, graffiti on walls spells out lyrics, and the world itself seems to be singing with him.

Some viewers read this as an exaggeration of Baby’s inner experience, but the phenomenological reading is more compelling. This is what it looks like when a body is completely attuned to its environment. For Merleau-Ponty, the lived body and the world are not separate entities that simply interact. The body is always already in the world, and the world resonates differently depending on one’s bodily orientation toward it. Baby’s walk is not a fantasy; rather, it is the film’s attempt to render the reciprocity between an attuned body and its surroundings.

The camera’s relationship to Baby is equally important. In conventional action films, the camera functions as an outside observer. It watches characters from a position of detachment, often from above or at a distance. Wright’s camera in Baby Driver does not do this. During the driving sequences, it moves with Baby, inhabiting his spatial perspective rather than merely observing it. There is no shaky-cam action or rapid editing designed to produce a sense of danger. Instead, Wright relies on long takes and fluid tracking shots to preserve the temporal continuity of Baby’s bodily experience.

In these sequences, the camera itself seems to become incorporated into Baby’s body-schema in much the same way as his car and his music. We do not simply watch him drive; for the duration of the shot, we occupy the passenger’s seat.

This formal choice has direct phenomenological consequences for the viewer. Merleau-Ponty argues that perception is not passive reception but active bodily participation. When we watch a skilled body in motion, we do not merely observe it; our own motor systems become involved. Our bodies respond to what is happening on screen in pre-reflective ways, and Wright’s style maximizes this effect. By refusing the detached observer’s perspective, he also refuses to give us a safe vantage point from which to view Baby’s embodiment from the outside. Instead, he places us inside—or as close to inside the film as cinema can manage.

Finally, the film’s treatment of diegetic and non-diegetic music is phenomenologically significant. In conventional films, music is typically extradiegetic, existing primarily for the audience’s emotional orientation rather than for the characters themselves. Baby’s music is different. It is diegetic: he hears it, and we hear exactly what he hears. As a result, the music is not simply commentary on Baby’s world; it is part of the world’s very structure and the medium through which his body-schema is organized.

When the earbuds come out, the film changes immediately. The silence is not peaceful but vulnerable and disorienting. Wright uses this contrast to emphasize the difference between an embodied and a disembodied mode of experience. With the music, Baby exists in a pre-reflective bodily mode. Without it, he is pushed into the reflective, conscious mode that Cartesian philosophy treats as the norm and that Merleau-Ponty argues is the exception.

Conclusion

Baby Driver is not simply a film about a man who is good at driving. It is a film about what it means to be a body moving through a world to which that body is completely attuned—and about what happens when that attunement is shaped by trauma and loss.

Merleau-Ponty provides the conceptual vocabulary to explain precisely what the film is doing: the body-schema that incorporates the car and music into a single perceptual system, the intentional arc that orients Baby toward action before deliberation, the sedimentation of a childhood crash into an ongoing bodily condition, and the pre-reflective flow of experience that Wright’s filmmaking produces in the viewer.

The pre-reflective body, the extended body-schema, and the sedimented history that shapes how a body moves through the world are not unique to Baby. They are features of embodied existence that every person encounters. Edgar Wright encountered them and made a film. Merleau-Ponty encountered them and wrote a philosophy.

The alignment between Wright’s filmmaking and Merleau-Ponty’s philosophy provides both a representation and a vocabulary for what it actually feels like to be a body moving through the world.
