Food Insecurity and Academic Performance Among College Students

Ryan Trudeau

Public Health
University of Utah

Public Health July 2026 12 min read

Abstract

To what extent do college students experience food insecurity, and how does it affect their academic performance? This proposed study examines that question to identify ways universities can reduce food insecurity on college campuses and improve student success. It hypothesizes that food insecurity negatively affects students’ psychosocial health—including depression, anxiety, and hope—which may then contribute to poorer academic performance.

The study proposes following incoming first-year students at the University of Utah throughout their undergraduate education. By collecting repeated measures of food security, psychosocial health, demographic characteristics, and grade point average, the research would assess both the prevalence of food insecurity and its relationship with academic outcomes over time. The findings could help universities develop more accessible food-assistance programs, reduce barriers to resource use, and better support students whose academic progress is affected by limited access to nutritious food.

Keywords

Food Insecurity, Public Health, Higher Education, College Students, Mental Health, Academic Performance, Nutrition, Health Policy, Research Methods, Social Determinants of Health

Literature Review

Food insecurity has become an increasingly significant concern on college campuses due to rising educational costs, limited financial assistance, and barriers that prevent students from accessing available resources. Food insecurity is defined as “limited or uncertain access to nutritionally adequate, safe, and acceptable foods that can be obtained in socially acceptable ways” (El Zein et al., 2019). As tuition, housing, and meal plan costs continue to outpace available financial aid, many students struggle to maintain consistent access to nutritious food (Raskind et al., 2019). This imbalance places college students among the populations most vulnerable to food insecurity, negatively affecting both their psychosocial well-being and academic success.

Previous research has demonstrated that federal food assistance programs improve food security and increase fruit and vegetable consumption among K–12 students. While the relationship between nutrition and mental health has been extensively studied in younger populations, researchers have only recently begun examining how food insecurity influences academic performance among college students (Raskind et al., 2019). This emerging body of research also explores the availability and effectiveness of campus food pantries as a strategy for improving student well-being.

Several demographic characteristics have been associated with a greater risk of food insecurity among college students. Students living on campus have been found to experience higher levels of food insecurity, while off-campus students are more likely to utilize campus food pantries (DeBate et al., 2021; Idehai et al., 2024). First-generation college students, along with Asian, Hispanic, and Pacific Islander students, also experience disproportionately high rates of food insecurity (Idehai et al., 2024). Additionally, Mirabitur (2016) found that food-insecure male students without access to a vehicle consumed the fewest fruits and vegetables on average. Identifying these at-risk populations is an important first step toward developing effective interventions.

Despite the availability of campus resources, many students face barriers that discourage them from seeking assistance. Meal plans are often prohibitively expensive for low-income students, yet approximately 70% of food-insecure students still purchase meal plans, suggesting that food quality and affordability remain concerns in addition to food quantity (El Zein et al., 2019). Social and structural stigma further discourages students from using campus food pantries. The competitive nature of college environments and the perception that food assistance is a “handout” often leave students feeling embarrassed to seek help (Idehai et al., 2024). Many students who could benefit from these resources also believe they are “not poor enough” to qualify, despite experiencing food insecurity.

Research suggests that universities can reduce these barriers by expanding access to campus food pantries, establishing satellite pickup locations, implementing online ordering systems, and increasing the availability of fresh produce and protein-rich foods. Studies also emphasize the importance of distinguishing between marginally food-secure and highly food-secure students. Mirabitur (2016) found that students experiencing marginal food insecurity consumed the fewest fruits and vegetables of any food security group. As food assistance policies become more restrictive, policymakers should recognize marginally food-secure students as an at-risk population rather than focusing exclusively on those experiencing severe food insecurity.

Although food insecurity may initially appear to be a financial or nutritional issue, its effects extend far beyond access to food. Drawing on Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs, researchers have linked food insecurity to depression, anxiety, hopelessness, poor sleep, and unhealthy eating behaviors (Raskind et al., 2019). Existing research suggests that psychosocial health may mediate the relationship between food insecurity and academic performance, although evidence of a direct relationship between food insecurity and GPA remains limited. Additional research is needed to better understand how food security influences academic success and to evaluate the effectiveness of interventions such as campus food pantries. Addressing these gaps may help universities develop policies that improve both student well-being and educational outcomes.

Methods

Data Collection

This study proposes that the University of Utah conduct a longitudinal cohort study examining the relationship between food security and academic performance among undergraduate students. Because publicly available datasets contain limited information specific to University of Utah students, the university would administer its own survey to collect original data.

The survey would gather demographic information, including age, gender, ethnicity, housing type, year in school, meal plan status, and employment status, in addition to measures of food security, psychosocial health, and grade point average (GPA). Using a quantitative approach, the study would evaluate the prevalence of food insecurity, its relationship with academic performance, and whether psychosocial health serves as a mediating factor between the two variables. All information would be self-reported, and surveys containing incomplete responses would be excluded from the final analysis.

Sample

Participants would consist of incoming first-year students at the University of Utah beginning in the Fall 2026 semester. Eligible participants must be between 18 and 25 years old and enrolled at least part-time.

Approximately 3,000 students would be randomly selected from an incoming class of roughly 5,000 first-year students using university email addresses provided by the Office of Undergraduate Admissions. A simple random sampling method would be used because it is efficient, minimizes selection bias, and provides a representative sample of the larger student population.

Selected students would first receive a baseline survey to determine eligibility. Those who qualify would then provide informed consent before participating in the longitudinal study. Based on response rates reported by Raskind et al. (2019), approximately 22.9% of invited students are expected to participate, resulting in roughly 687 completed surveys.

To encourage participation, students who complete each survey would be entered into a raffle to win one of three $30 Amazon gift cards. To improve long-term retention, raffle prizes would increase by $10 during each subsequent six-month survey period.

Participants would complete surveys beginning in August 2026, before starting college, and every six months thereafter until Spring 2030, when most are expected to graduate. Beginning data collection before students enter college allows researchers to establish baseline food security levels while students are still living at home, providing an important comparison point for future measurements.

Following students throughout their undergraduate careers offers a more complete understanding of how food insecurity changes over time and how those changes relate to psychosocial health and academic performance. Because the sample is selected through simple random sampling, the findings should be generalizable to future incoming first-year students at the University of Utah. However, the results would not necessarily apply to students at other institutions or to individuals outside higher education. The selected instruments have all been validated in previous research and are widely recognized as reliable measures of food security and psychosocial health.

Measures

Food Security. Food insecurity will be defined as “limited or uncertain access to nutritionally adequate, safe, and acceptable foods that can be obtained in socially acceptable ways” (El Zein et al., 2019). To measure food security, this study will use the United States Department of Agriculture (USDA) 6-Item Short Form Food Security Survey Module, a widely used and validated instrument for assessing household food security.

Participants will respond to questions regarding their access to food during the previous twelve months. One example asks: “In the last 12 months, did you ever cut the size of your meals or skip meals because there wasn’t enough money for food?”

Responses generate a score ranging from 0 to 6, with higher scores indicating greater food insecurity. Following USDA guidelines, scores will be categorized as:

  • 0–1: High or marginal food security
  • 2–4: Low food security
  • 5–6: Very low food security (DeBate et al., 2021)

Psychosocial Health. Psychosocial health will be measured using three indicators: depression, anxiety, and hope. Higher depression and anxiety scores indicate poorer psychosocial health, while higher hope scores indicate more positive psychosocial well-being.

Depression will be assessed using the Patient Health Questionnaire-9 (PHQ-9). Participants will report how frequently they experienced nine symptoms of depression during the previous two weeks. Example items include “Little interest or pleasure in doing things” and “Trouble falling or staying asleep.” Responses range from 0 (“Not at all”) to 3 (“Nearly every day”), producing a total score between 0 and 27 (Raskind et al., 2019).

Anxiety will be measured using the Anxiety Sensitivity Index-3 (ASI-3). This eighteen-item questionnaire evaluates participants’ responses to anxiety-related sensations, such as chest tightness, fear, and physiological arousal. Response options range from 0 (“Very little”) to 4 (“Very much”), with total scores ranging from 0 to 72 (Raskind et al., 2019).

Hope will be measured using the Adult State Hope Scale, a six-item instrument that assesses participants’ confidence in achieving personal goals. Statements such as “At the present time, I am energetically pursuing my goals” and “There are lots of ways around any problem that I am facing right now” are rated on an eight-point scale from 1 (“Definitely false”) to 8 (“Definitely true”). Scores range from 6 to 48, with higher scores reflecting greater levels of hope (Raskind et al., 2019).

Academic Performance. Academic performance will be measured using each participant’s self-reported grade point average (GPA) on a standard 4.0 scale.

Demographic Variables. Several demographic variables will be collected as covariates, including gender, age, race/ethnicity, housing type, meal plan status, and employment status. Housing type will be categorized as on-campus, off-campus with parents, or off-campus without parents. These demographic variables will be recorded during each six-month survey to account for changes over time and to better understand factors that may influence food insecurity and academic performance.
