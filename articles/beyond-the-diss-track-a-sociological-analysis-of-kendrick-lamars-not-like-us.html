Beyond the Diss Track: A Sociological Analysis of Kendrick Lamar's Not Like Us

Luke Marshall

Sociology
University of Oregon

Cultural Analysis July 2026 14 min read

Abstract

This article examines Kendrick Lamar's 2024 song Not Like Us through a sociological lens. By analyzing themes of social stratification, social mobility, race, ethnicity, cultural appropriation, and colonialism, the article explores how contemporary hip-hop can serve as a form of social commentary. Rather than focusing on the celebrity dispute itself, the analysis demonstrates how popular music reflects broader debates surrounding identity, authenticity, power, and cultural ownership.

Keywords

Sociology, Popular Culture, Hip-Hop, Kendrick Lamar, Drake, Race, Ethnicity, Social Stratification, Social Mobility, Cultural Appropriation, Colonialism, Identity, Culture, Music Sociology

Introduction

Popular music has long served as more than a form of entertainment. Throughout history, artists have used music to critique social institutions, challenge political systems, and examine issues of identity, inequality, and culture. Within hip-hop in particular, lyrical storytelling has frequently addressed topics such as poverty, race, class, violence, and social mobility. As a result, contemporary rap music provides a valuable lens through which sociological concepts can be examined and discussed.

In 2024, Kendrick Lamar released Not Like Us during a highly publicized feud with fellow rapper Drake. Although widely recognized as a diss track, the song extends beyond personal criticism by presenting arguments about authenticity, cultural identity, social class, and the commercialization of Black culture. Rather than focusing solely on Drake as an individual, Lamar uses the conflict to raise broader questions regarding who is perceived as an authentic representative of a community and how social background influences public identity.

Many of the themes explored in Not Like Us closely align with concepts commonly studied in sociology. The song raises questions about social stratification, social mobility, race, ethnicity, and cultural appropriation, while also drawing historical comparisons that invoke discussions of colonialism and the unequal distribution of power. Although the song is rooted in contemporary popular culture, its arguments reflect broader sociological debates surrounding identity formation, cultural ownership, and structural inequality.

This article analyzes Not Like Us through a sociological perspective by examining how Kendrick Lamar critiques class differences, questions cultural authenticity, and frames Drake's relationship with Black American culture through concepts of colonialism and cultural appropriation. Rather than evaluating the personal dispute itself, this analysis focuses on how the song illustrates sociological theories regarding social identity, power, and cultural belonging.

Music as Social Commentary

Hip-hop has historically functioned as a platform for social commentary, allowing artists to communicate experiences with inequality, discrimination, violence, and systemic barriers. Rather than serving solely as entertainment, rap music often reflects the lived realities of the communities from which it emerges. Sociologists frequently view popular music as a cultural product that both shapes and reflects society by influencing public conversations surrounding politics, race, class, and identity.

Kendrick Lamar's work has consistently embodied this tradition. Throughout his career, he has explored themes including poverty, institutional racism, community violence, and personal identity, often drawing upon his upbringing in Compton, California. His lyrics frequently extend beyond autobiographical storytelling to critique broader social structures and historical inequalities. In this context, Not Like Us represents more than a personal dispute between two successful musicians. Instead, it uses a widely publicized conflict to question issues of authenticity, cultural belonging, and the commercialization of Black culture within the music industry.

The public nature of Lamar's dispute with Drake contributed to the song's widespread cultural significance. Millions of listeners engaged with the lyrics not only as entertainment but also as commentary on broader questions of identity and representation. The song became a focal point for discussions surrounding who can authentically represent a culture, whether personal background influences artistic credibility, and how commercial success intersects with cultural identity. These debates illustrate how popular music can become a vehicle for examining larger sociological questions that extend far beyond the individuals involved.

Viewed through a sociological lens, Not Like Us demonstrates that music can serve as a form of cultural criticism. Rather than presenting abstract theories, Lamar communicates complex ideas through narrative, symbolism, and historical references that encourage audiences to consider how power, identity, and inequality continue to influence contemporary society. These themes provide the foundation for understanding the song's broader arguments regarding class, social mobility, and cultural authenticity.

Class, Social Mobility, and Authenticity

One of the central sociological themes in Not Like Us is the relationship between social class and cultural authenticity. Throughout the song, Kendrick Lamar argues that an individual's upbringing shapes their lived experiences and influences how they are perceived within a cultural community. Rather than focusing solely on wealth or success, Lamar emphasizes the importance of social background in constructing identity.

This distinction becomes clearer when comparing the childhoods of Lamar and Drake. Lamar was raised in Compton, California, a city that has long been associated with concentrated poverty, gang violence, and economic disadvantage. His family relied on public assistance while he grew up in an environment where violence and financial insecurity were common realities. These experiences became central to his music and public identity. In contrast, Drake was raised in Toronto, Canada, in a comparatively stable middle-class environment. Although he experienced personal hardships, including his parents' divorce, he entered the entertainment industry as a teenager through an acting career before achieving success as a recording artist.

From a sociological perspective, these different upbringings illustrate the concept of social stratification, or the hierarchical organization of society based on factors such as income, education, occupation, and access to opportunities. Individuals born into different social classes often encounter unequal access to resources that influence educational attainment, professional networks, and career opportunities. These structural differences shape life experiences long before individuals reach adulthood.

Lamar's criticism extends beyond Drake's financial success. Instead, he questions whether someone whose life experiences differ substantially from those commonly associated with Black American urban communities can authentically represent those experiences in music. His argument suggests that cultural credibility is influenced not only by race but also by lived experience, neighborhood environment, and class background. In this interpretation, authenticity becomes a social construct shaped by shared experiences rather than simply personal identification with a community.

The comparison also highlights the distinction between ascribed status and achieved status, two foundational sociological concepts. Ascribed status refers to characteristics individuals are born into, such as family background or socioeconomic class, while achieved status reflects accomplishments earned throughout life. Lamar presents himself as someone who achieved success despite beginning in poverty, whereas he argues that Drake benefited from opportunities associated with a comparatively more privileged upbringing. Whether listeners agree with this interpretation or not, the contrast illustrates how debates over authenticity often involve broader questions about class, opportunity, and the role of lived experience in shaping personal identity.

Colonialism and Cultural Appropriation

Beyond questions of social class, Not Like Us introduces another sociological theme by connecting contemporary debates over cultural authenticity to the concepts of colonialism and cultural appropriation. Throughout the song, Kendrick Lamar argues that Drake has benefited from adopting elements of Black American culture—particularly the musical traditions associated with Atlanta's hip-hop scene—without sharing the same cultural experiences from which those traditions emerged. While this comparison is intentionally provocative, it raises broader sociological questions about who benefits from cultural exchange and where the line exists between appreciation and appropriation.

Historically, colonialism refers to the domination of one society by another through political control, economic exploitation, and the extraction of resources. Although Lamar references this historical concept, his argument is symbolic rather than literal. He suggests that modern forms of cultural influence can mirror historical power dynamics when individuals profit from communities whose cultural practices they did not help create. In sociology and cultural studies, these discussions frequently overlap with the concept of cultural appropriation, which describes the adoption of cultural symbols, traditions, or expressions by members of another group in ways that may overlook or minimize their original historical and social context.

Lamar develops this argument by referencing Drake's collaborations with several prominent Atlanta artists, including Future, Lil Baby, 21 Savage, Young Thug, Quavo, and 2 Chainz. He argues that these relationships contributed to Drake's credibility within Black American hip-hop culture while simultaneously expanding his commercial success. From Lamar's perspective, these collaborations represent more than artistic partnerships; they illustrate how cultural capital can be acquired through association with communities that possess social and cultural influence.

From a sociological standpoint, however, this interpretation is open to debate. Cultural exchange has long been a defining characteristic of music, particularly within hip-hop, where collaboration across geographic regions and cultural backgrounds is common. Some scholars argue that such collaborations promote innovation and mutual influence rather than exploitation. Others contend that unequal economic and social power can allow certain artists to profit disproportionately from cultural traditions that originated within marginalized communities. Lamar's criticism reflects this latter perspective by framing cultural authenticity as inseparable from historical experience and community membership.

Whether one agrees with Lamar's conclusion or not, Not Like Us illustrates how popular music can serve as a platform for broader discussions about cultural ownership, identity, and power. The song encourages listeners to consider not only who creates culture but also who benefits from it, making debates over cultural appropriation an important sociological lens through which contemporary music and media can be analyzed.

Race, Ethnicity, and Identity

While Not Like Us frequently references race, Kendrick Lamar's argument extends beyond racial identity alone. Instead, the song emphasizes the distinction between race and ethnicity, two concepts that sociologists recognize as related but fundamentally different. Race generally refers to socially constructed categories based on perceived physical characteristics, whereas ethnicity encompasses shared cultural traditions, history, language, and collective identity. Understanding this distinction is essential to interpreting Lamar's broader critique.

Drake, whose father is Black and mother is white, identifies as a Black artist. Lamar does not dispute Drake's racial identity. Rather, his criticism centers on what he views as differences in cultural experience and socialization. According to Lamar's interpretation, growing up in a middle-class Canadian environment differs significantly from being raised in communities where many aspects of Black American culture—including those reflected in hip-hop—were shaped by historical discrimination, economic inequality, and systemic barriers. His argument suggests that cultural identity is influenced not only by ancestry but also by shared lived experiences.

This distinction reflects broader sociological discussions about identity formation. Individual identity develops through interactions with family, neighborhoods, schools, peer groups, and social institutions. People with similar racial backgrounds may develop different cultural identities depending on where they are raised, the communities in which they participate, and the social experiences that shape their worldview. Consequently, questions of authenticity often involve far more than biological ancestry or self-identification.

Lamar also criticizes what he perceives as Drake's adoption of narratives associated with violence and street life despite having experienced a substantially different upbringing. From Lamar's perspective, presenting these experiences as personal realities risks blurring the line between representation and performance. This criticism aligns with sociological discussions surrounding symbolic authenticity, in which audiences evaluate whether public identities accurately reflect an individual's lived experiences or primarily serve commercial purposes.

Ultimately, Not Like Us demonstrates how debates surrounding race, ethnicity, and authenticity are rarely straightforward. Rather than offering simple answers, the song illustrates how identity is shaped by an intersection of race, class, culture, history, and personal experience. Whether listeners agree with Lamar's conclusions or not, the discussion highlights the complexity of identity in contemporary society and shows why sociological concepts remain valuable tools for understanding cultural conflicts that extend far beyond the music itself.

Limitations of the Analysis

Although Not Like Us provides a compelling lens through which to examine sociological concepts, it is important to recognize the limitations of interpreting a work of popular music as a source of sociological evidence. Kendrick Lamar's lyrics represent an artistic perspective shaped by the context of a highly publicized personal conflict. As with many forms of artistic expression, the song employs exaggeration, symbolism, and rhetorical devices that are intended to persuade audiences rather than present an objective account of events. Consequently, its claims should be understood as cultural commentary rather than established sociological fact.

Many of the song's central arguments—particularly those concerning cultural appropriation and authenticity—remain subjects of ongoing academic and public debate. Some scholars argue that cultural exchange is an inevitable and often beneficial feature of increasingly interconnected societies, fostering collaboration and innovation across cultural boundaries. Others contend that cultural borrowing becomes problematic when individuals or institutions with greater social or economic power disproportionately profit from traditions that originated within historically marginalized communities. Both perspectives contribute to broader sociological discussions about identity, representation, and power.

The analysis presented in this article focuses specifically on Lamar's interpretation of these issues rather than attempting to determine whether his criticisms of Drake are objectively correct. Drake and his supporters may interpret the same events differently, emphasizing artistic collaboration, globalization, and the evolving nature of cultural identity. Recognizing these competing viewpoints is essential to maintaining a balanced sociological analysis.

Ultimately, sociology seeks to understand how individuals construct meaning within society rather than simply determining who is right or wrong in a cultural dispute. Not Like Us serves as a valuable case study because it illustrates how contemporary music can generate meaningful conversations about class, ethnicity, authenticity, and cultural ownership. Regardless of one's opinion of the artists involved, the song demonstrates the continued relevance of sociological concepts in interpreting modern popular culture.

Conclusion

Not Like Us demonstrates that popular music can function as more than entertainment by providing a platform for examining complex social issues. Although the song emerged from a highly publicized dispute between Kendrick Lamar and Drake, its broader significance lies in the sociological questions it raises regarding class, identity, authenticity, and cultural ownership. Rather than simply criticizing another artist, Lamar uses the conflict to challenge audiences to consider how social background, lived experience, and historical context influence the ways individuals construct and perform identity.

Examining the song through concepts such as social stratification, social mobility, race, ethnicity, colonialism, and cultural appropriation demonstrates how contemporary cultural products can reflect longstanding sociological debates. Whether discussing differences in upbringing, the commercialization of culture, or competing ideas of authenticity, Not Like Us illustrates how music often serves as a reflection of broader social structures and power relationships.

At the same time, the song highlights the importance of approaching cultural criticism with nuance. Many of the issues Lamar raises remain contested among scholars and the public, particularly regarding the boundaries between cultural appreciation, collaboration, and appropriation. Because identity is shaped by numerous intersecting social factors, there are rarely simple answers to questions surrounding authenticity and belonging. Recognizing these complexities allows for a more balanced sociological interpretation while acknowledging the diversity of perspectives that exist within contemporary society.

Ultimately, Not Like Us serves as a compelling case study of how sociology can be applied to modern popular culture. By connecting a contemporary musical work to foundational sociological concepts, the song demonstrates that discussions of class, race, ethnicity, and power extend far beyond academic textbooks. Popular music continues to shape public discourse, making it a valuable medium through which students and scholars can explore the evolving relationship between culture and society.
