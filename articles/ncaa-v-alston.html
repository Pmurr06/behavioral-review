NCAA v. Alston: The Supreme Court Decision That Changed College Athletics

David Morgan

Business Administration & Management student at Washington State University

Case Analysis June 2026 10 min read

Keywords

NCAA, Antitrust Law, Student-Athletes, College Athletics, Sherman Antitrust Act, Education Benefits, Sports Law

Introduction

For decades, college athletics operated under a model unlike almost any other industry in the United States. Universities generated billions of dollars through television contracts, sponsorships, ticket sales, and merchandise, while the athletes responsible for much of that revenue remained subject to strict NCAA rules limiting the benefits they could receive. Supporters of the system argued that amateurism distinguished college sports from professional athletics and preserved the educational mission of universities. Critics, however, questioned whether those restrictions were fair in an era when college athletics had become a multibillion-dollar enterprise.

The debate reached the United States Supreme Court in NCAA v. Alston (2021), a landmark case examining whether certain NCAA rules violated federal antitrust law. Although the decision focused specifically on education-related benefits rather than unrestricted athlete compensation, it marked one of the most significant legal challenges to the NCAA's longstanding model of amateurism. The Court's unanimous opinion reaffirmed that the NCAA is not exempt from the same competition laws that govern other organizations and businesses operating in the American economy.

The case also arrived during a period of growing public debate over the rights of student-athletes. Questions surrounding scholarships, name, image, and likeness (NIL), revenue sharing, and athlete welfare had become increasingly prominent as college sports continued to expand financially. While Alston did not resolve every issue surrounding athlete compensation, it fundamentally changed the legal landscape by limiting the NCAA's ability to restrict certain education-related benefits and signaling greater judicial scrutiny of its compensation rules.

This article examines the background of NCAA v. Alston, the Supreme Court's reasoning, and why the decision continues to shape the future of college athletics. By exploring both the legal arguments and the broader policy implications, the case demonstrates how antitrust law has become one of the most influential forces in redefining the relationship between universities, athletic organizations, and the student-athletes who drive the success of collegiate sports.

The Road to NCAA v. Alston

For much of its history, the National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA) defended its rules by arguing that college athletes should remain amateurs rather than professionals. According to the NCAA, limiting compensation preserved the educational purpose of college athletics and distinguished collegiate competition from professional sports. Student-athletes could receive scholarships covering tuition, housing, meals, and other educational expenses, but many additional forms of compensation were prohibited under NCAA rules.

As college sports became increasingly profitable, however, criticism of this system intensified. Universities and athletic conferences signed television contracts worth billions of dollars, coaches received multi-million-dollar salaries, and merchandising and sponsorship revenues continued to grow. Meanwhile, the athletes whose performances generated much of this revenue remained subject to strict restrictions on the benefits they could receive.

In 2014, a group of Division I football and basketball players filed antitrust lawsuits challenging several NCAA compensation rules. The athletes argued that restrictions on education-related benefits violated the Sherman Antitrust Act, a federal law enacted to promote fair competition and prevent organizations from engaging in unreasonable restraints of trade. They contended that the NCAA's rules artificially limited the benefits schools could offer prospective athletes, reducing competition among universities and preventing student-athletes from receiving educational opportunities that would otherwise be available in a competitive marketplace.

The litigation eventually reached the United States District Court for the Northern District of California. After reviewing extensive testimony and economic evidence, the district court concluded that the NCAA's restrictions on education-related benefits violated federal antitrust law. The court ordered the NCAA to permit member schools to provide additional education-related benefits, including graduate school scholarships, postgraduate internships, computers, scientific equipment, tutoring, and certain academic achievement awards.

The NCAA appealed the ruling, arguing that allowing greater compensation would undermine the concept of amateurism and diminish consumer interest in college sports. The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed the district court's decision, finding that the restrictions unnecessarily limited competition while still allowing the NCAA to preserve many aspects of amateur athletics.

Unwilling to accept that outcome, the NCAA petitioned the United States Supreme Court. The case presented an important legal question: could the NCAA continue restricting education-related benefits under the banner of amateurism, or were those restrictions an unlawful restraint of trade under the Sherman Antitrust Act?

The answer would not only determine the future of education-related compensation but would also shape the broader legal relationship between college athletics and federal antitrust law. As public debate surrounding student-athlete rights continued to grow, many observers recognized that the Court's decision had the potential to redefine the balance between preserving amateur competition and ensuring fair treatment for the athletes who make college sports possible.

The Supreme Court's Decision

In June 2021, the United States Supreme Court unanimously ruled against the NCAA in NCAA v. Alston, affirming the lower courts' decisions that certain NCAA restrictions on education-related benefits violated the Sherman Antitrust Act. Justice Neil Gorsuch authored the Court's opinion, concluding that the NCAA was not entitled to special treatment simply because it governed college athletics. Like other organizations operating within the marketplace, the NCAA remained subject to federal antitrust law.

One of the NCAA's primary arguments was that restricting athlete compensation preserved the tradition of amateur college sports. According to the organization, consumers valued college athletics because athletes competed as students rather than professionals. The NCAA argued that allowing schools to provide additional benefits could blur that distinction and ultimately reduce fan interest.

The Court acknowledged the importance of preserving the unique character of college athletics but concluded that this objective did not automatically justify restrictions that limited competition among universities. After reviewing the evidence presented during the trial, the justices agreed that the NCAA had failed to demonstrate why limits on education-related benefits were necessary to maintain consumer demand. As a result, the Court held that these restrictions unlawfully restrained competition between member institutions.

Importantly, the decision did not require schools to pay unlimited salaries to student-athletes or eliminate all NCAA compensation rules. Instead, the ruling focused specifically on education-related benefits, allowing colleges and universities to compete by offering resources such as graduate school scholarships, academic awards, tutoring, computers, internships, and other educational opportunities that directly supported student success.

Although the decision itself was relatively narrow, its significance extended far beyond education-related benefits. By unanimously rejecting the NCAA's argument for broad immunity from antitrust scrutiny, the Supreme Court established that the organization's compensation rules could be challenged under the same legal standards applied to other industries. Many legal scholars viewed the ruling as an indication that additional restrictions on athlete compensation could face similar challenges in future litigation.

Justice Brett Kavanaugh's concurring opinion attracted particular attention. While agreeing with the Court's decision, he wrote separately to question the NCAA's entire compensation model. Kavanaugh argued that many of the NCAA's remaining restrictions would likely be considered illegal in nearly any other industry because businesses generally cannot agree to limit worker compensation while generating billions of dollars in revenue. Although his concurrence did not change the Court's holding, it signaled growing judicial skepticism toward the NCAA's longstanding business model and suggested that future legal challenges could continue reshaping college athletics.

The unanimous nature of the decision also carried symbolic importance. Rather than reflecting ideological divisions, all nine justices agreed that the NCAA's education-related compensation restrictions violated federal antitrust law. The decision reinforced the principle that organizations cannot avoid competition laws simply because they operate within the context of amateur sports. Instead, courts must evaluate whether restrictions genuinely promote competition or merely protect existing economic arrangements.

In many respects, NCAA v. Alston represented more than a dispute over educational benefits. It marked a turning point in the legal relationship between universities, athletic organizations, and student-athletes, opening the door to broader conversations about fairness, compensation, and the future structure of college sports.

Why the Decision Still Matters

The significance of NCAA v. Alston extends well beyond the education-related benefits that were directly at issue before the Supreme Court. In many ways, the case represents a shift in how courts, universities, and the public view the relationship between student-athletes and the institutions they represent. For decades, the NCAA successfully defended its compensation model by emphasizing amateurism. Following Alston, however, that justification no longer appears immune from legal scrutiny.

One of the most interesting aspects of the decision is that it forced people to reconsider what fairness means in college athletics. Universities invest enormous resources into their athletic programs because successful teams generate revenue, increase alumni engagement, and strengthen institutional visibility. Football and men's basketball programs, in particular, often produce millions of dollars through television contracts, sponsorships, ticket sales, merchandise, and postseason appearances. Student-athletes are central to that success, yet for many years the benefits they could receive remained tightly restricted.

At the same time, the opposing argument deserves serious consideration. College athletes often receive scholarships worth tens of thousands of dollars, along with access to tutoring, academic support, housing, meals, medical care, and training facilities. Many people believe those opportunities represent substantial compensation on their own and argue that transforming college athletics into a professional marketplace could undermine the educational mission that distinguishes college sports from professional leagues.

I understand both perspectives, but I believe the Supreme Court reached the correct decision. The Court did not eliminate amateur athletics or require universities to pay unlimited salaries. Instead, it recognized that schools should be able to compete by offering meaningful education-related benefits without violating NCAA rules. Allowing graduate scholarships, internships, academic awards, and other educational opportunities benefits student-athletes while remaining consistent with the broader purpose of higher education.

The decision also reflected an important legal principle. Organizations with significant market power cannot simply avoid antitrust law by claiming that longstanding traditions justify restrictions on competition. While traditions are important, they must still operate within the framework of federal law. The Supreme Court's unanimous ruling reinforced that no organization, including the NCAA, is automatically exempt from those principles.

Although Alston addressed only education-related benefits, its influence has extended much further. The decision was issued during a period when public support for expanding athlete rights was already increasing. Shortly afterward, the NCAA adopted an interim policy allowing athletes to profit from their name, image, and likeness (NIL) under state laws and institutional policies. While NIL rights did not result directly from the Court's holding, the broader legal environment created by Alston accelerated conversations about athlete compensation and contributed to one of the most significant periods of change in the history of college athletics.

As new legal challenges continue to emerge, universities, conferences, and lawmakers will likely face difficult questions regarding revenue sharing, athlete employment status, transfer rules, and the future governance of college sports. Whether those changes ultimately strengthen or weaken collegiate athletics remains uncertain. What is clear, however, is that NCAA v. Alston marked the beginning of a new chapter in the legal and economic relationship between student-athletes and the institutions they represent.

Conclusion

The Supreme Court's decision in NCAA v. Alston represents one of the most influential legal developments in the modern history of college athletics. Although the case focused specifically on education-related benefits, its broader impact has reshaped discussions surrounding athlete compensation, amateurism, and the future of collegiate sports. By unanimously concluding that the NCAA's compensation rules are subject to federal antitrust law, the Court reinforced the principle that even long-established organizations must operate within the boundaries of fair competition.

The ruling also demonstrated that preserving tradition cannot, by itself, justify restrictions that limit competition or opportunities for student-athletes. While the NCAA continues to play an important role in organizing collegiate athletics, its authority is no longer viewed as beyond judicial review. Instead, future policies will likely face increasing scrutiny as courts, lawmakers, and universities continue evaluating how best to balance educational values with the commercial realities of modern college sports.

As the landscape continues to evolve through new legislation, NIL developments, and additional legal challenges, NCAA v. Alston will remain a foundational case for understanding the rights of student-athletes and the responsibilities of athletic organizations. The decision did not answer every question surrounding compensation in college athletics, but it fundamentally changed the conversation. For students studying business, law, public policy, or sports management, the case serves as a reminder that legal decisions often influence industries far beyond the courtroom, shaping the future of organizations, markets, and the people they affect.

Ultimately, NCAA v. Alston illustrates how constitutional principles, federal antitrust law, and economic realities intersect within one of America's most recognizable institutions. Its legacy will continue to influence the governance of college athletics for years to come, making it an essential case for anyone seeking to understand the changing relationship between education, competition, and commerce.
