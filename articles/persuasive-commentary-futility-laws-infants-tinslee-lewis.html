Persuasive Commentary: Futility Laws on Infants and the Case of Tinslee Lewis

Katie Nguyen

Health Sciences
University of South Florida

Public Policy July 2026 8 min read

Background: The Case of Tinslee Lewis

Medical decisions involving critically ill children are among the most difficult ethical issues faced by families and healthcare professionals. Questions surrounding life-sustaining treatment often involve balancing medical expertise, parental authority, and the child's best interests. One case that brought these issues into the national spotlight was that of Tinslee Lewis, an infant whose treatment sparked widespread debate over medical futility laws in Texas.

Tinslee Lewis was born prematurely with Ebstein anomaly, a rare congenital heart defect that required extensive medical treatment and long-term life support. As her condition continued, physicians at Cook Children's Hospital in Fort Worth determined that continued life-sustaining treatment was medically futile and sought to discontinue life support under Texas law. This decision was strongly opposed by Tinslee's mother, Trinity Lewis, who believed her daughter should remain on life support and fought the hospital's decision through the court system. According to the Fort Worth Star-Telegram, Trinity argued that physicians should not have the legal authority to make an irreversible decision regarding her daughter's life without her consent (Johnson, 2019).

The Tinslee Lewis case quickly became more than a disagreement between one family and one hospital. It developed into a national discussion about who should have the final authority when parents and physicians disagree over continued medical treatment. While healthcare professionals have an ethical obligation to avoid unnecessary suffering, parents also have a deeply personal responsibility to advocate for what they believe is in their child's best interest. This conflict continues to raise important questions about medical ethics, parental rights, and the role of state law in end-of-life decision-making for children.

The Problem: Who Should Have the Final Decision?

The Tinslee Lewis case sparked widespread debate among healthcare professionals, legal scholars, and families because it raises one fundamental question: who should have the final authority when parents and physicians disagree about continuing life-sustaining treatment?

Under Texas's medical futility law, physicians and hospitals may discontinue life-sustaining treatment if they determine that further treatment is medically inappropriate after following the state's review process. According to Drabiak's overview of Texas futility law, the physician and hospital ultimately hold the authority to make the final decision when an agreement cannot be reached (Drabiak, n.d.). While this law was created to help physicians avoid providing treatments they believe offer no meaningful medical benefit, it has also generated significant ethical controversy.

Many people believe this approach gives healthcare providers too much authority over deeply personal family decisions. Parents often know their child better than anyone else and may believe there is still hope for recovery, even when physicians have reached a different conclusion. Others argue that medical professionals have the education and experience necessary to determine when treatment is no longer benefiting the patient and may only prolong suffering. Both perspectives are rooted in a desire to protect the child's well-being, yet they arrive at very different conclusions about who should make these life-altering decisions.

The disagreement between Trinity Lewis and Cook Children's Hospital illustrates just how difficult these situations can become. Trinity believed her daughter deserved every opportunity to continue fighting, while the hospital believed additional treatment would only prolong suffering without improving her condition. Cases like this demonstrate why medical futility laws remain one of the most debated issues in healthcare ethics, as they require balancing parental rights, medical judgment, and the best interests of the child.

Analysis: Should Parents Have the Final Authority?

Based on the facts of the Tinslee Lewis case, it is understandable why many families question whether Texas's medical futility law gives physicians too much authority over decisions involving critically ill children. While physicians have extensive medical knowledge and experience, parents are often the people who know their child best and are emotionally invested in pursuing every possible opportunity for recovery. When these two perspectives conflict, determining who should have the final decision becomes one of the most difficult ethical questions in healthcare.

From my perspective, parents should retain the primary authority to make decisions regarding life-sustaining treatment for their children whenever possible. Decisions involving the life of a child carry profound emotional, moral, and personal significance that cannot be measured solely through medical data. Although physicians are responsible for providing honest medical recommendations, parents should have the opportunity to carefully consider those recommendations and decide what they believe is in the best interest of their child.

At the same time, parental authority should not be unlimited. Physicians also have an ethical obligation to prevent unnecessary suffering and avoid treatments that may cause more harm than benefit. If medical professionals can clearly demonstrate, using credible medical evidence, that continuing treatment would only prolong pain with no reasonable possibility of improvement, their concerns should carry significant weight. Rather than allowing either parents or physicians to hold complete authority, the strongest approach is one that requires both sides to present evidence before life-sustaining treatment is withdrawn.

The difference between Texas and Florida law highlights two very different approaches to these situations. Under Texas law, physicians may ultimately have the authority to discontinue treatment after completing the legal review process. In contrast, Florida generally provides parents with greater decision-making authority regarding their child's medical care. I believe Florida's approach better respects the role of parents while still allowing physicians to provide professional guidance. However, safeguards should exist to ensure that continued treatment does not cause unnecessary suffering when overwhelming medical evidence shows that recovery is no longer possible.

The Tinslee Lewis case demonstrates why these laws continue to generate debate across the United States. Both Trinity Lewis and her daughter's physicians believed they were acting in Tinslee's best interest, yet they reached completely different conclusions. Cases like this remind us that healthcare ethics often involves balancing compassion, medical expertise, parental rights, and the well-being of the patient rather than relying on a single perspective alone.

Toward a Better Process for Families and Physicians

If policymakers want to reduce conflict in future cases, laws should require stronger communication and clearer procedural safeguards before life-sustaining treatment can be withdrawn over parental objection. Families should receive timely notice, plain-language explanations of medical findings, and meaningful opportunities to seek second opinions from independent specialists.

Hospitals should likewise be expected to document how they evaluated the child's best interests, including prognosis, pain burden, and available alternatives. Independent ethics review should be transparent and should include clinicians who are not directly involved in the case. Most importantly, the process should prioritize transfer options whenever possible so that families who disagree with one medical team can pursue care elsewhere without unnecessary delay. Building a structure around communication, evidence, and accountability would not eliminate disagreement, but it would make difficult decisions more ethically defensible and more humane for everyone involved.

Conclusion

The case of Tinslee Lewis demonstrates how difficult medical futility cases can be for both families and healthcare professionals. Decisions involving life-sustaining treatment are rarely straightforward, especially when parents and physicians sincerely disagree about what is in a child's best interest. While physicians have the medical expertise to evaluate a patient's condition, parents also have a unique responsibility to advocate for their child's life and well-being.

In my view, parents should continue to have the primary role in making these decisions whenever possible. However, this authority should be accompanied by careful consideration of medical evidence and the recommendations provided by healthcare professionals. Rather than allowing either side to make the final decision without accountability, policies should encourage collaboration and require both physicians and families to clearly justify their positions when disagreements arise.

Florida's approach provides parents with greater decision-making authority than Texas's medical futility law, and I believe it offers a stronger foundation for protecting family autonomy. At the same time, physicians should have the opportunity to demonstrate when continued treatment would likely cause unnecessary suffering or provide no meaningful medical benefit. Creating a process that values both parental rights and medical expertise can help ensure that decisions are made with the child's best interests at the center.

Although there may never be a perfect solution to cases like Tinslee Lewis's, this debate highlights the importance of developing healthcare policies that are both compassionate and ethically sound. By encouraging cooperation between families and healthcare providers rather than placing complete authority in one party's hands, future policies can better protect patients while respecting the difficult decisions faced by everyone involved.
