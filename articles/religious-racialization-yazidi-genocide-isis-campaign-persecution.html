Religious Racialization and the Yazidi Genocide: Examining ISIS's Campaign of Persecution

Ella Roehl

Human Physiology student at the University of Iowa

Global & International Affairs July 2026 8 min read

Introduction

The 2014 genocide of the Yazidi people by the Islamic State of Iraq and Syria (ISIS), also known as ISIL, represents one of the most devastating acts of religious persecution in the twenty-first century. Beginning on August 3, 2014, ISIS launched a coordinated attack against Yazidi communities centered around the Sinjar region of northern Iraq, carrying out mass executions, forced displacement, sexual slavery, and the systematic destruction of religious and cultural sites. Thousands of Yazidis were killed, while thousands more were abducted or forced to flee their homes in one of the largest humanitarian crises of the conflict in Iraq.

Although the violence was driven by extremist religious ideology, many scholars argue that ISIS's persecution of the Yazidis also reflected a broader process of religious racialization. Religious racialization occurs when a religious community becomes viewed as a fixed and inferior group based on beliefs, ancestry, or cultural identity rather than solely on religious practice. Through this process, the Yazidis were portrayed not simply as individuals practicing a different faith, but as an entire population that ISIS believed should be eliminated.

This article examines how religious racialization contributed to the Yazidi genocide by exploring ISIS's ideological beliefs, the historical marginalization of the Yazidi people, and the methods used to eradicate their religious identity. Understanding the relationship between religious extremism and racialization provides valuable insight into how identity-based violence develops and why protecting vulnerable religious minorities remains a critical international human rights issue.

The Yazidi People and a History of Religious Persecution

To understand the events of 2014, it is important to first understand who the Yazidis are and why they have historically been vulnerable to persecution. The Yazidis are a small ethnoreligious minority whose population is concentrated primarily in northern Iraq, particularly around the Sinjar Mountains, with smaller communities in Syria, Turkey, Armenia, and throughout the global diaspora. Their religion is one of the oldest monotheistic faiths in the Middle East and incorporates elements of ancient Mesopotamian beliefs, Zoroastrianism, Christianity, and Islam. Because the Yazidi faith is traditionally passed through family lineage and does not encourage religious conversion, the community has maintained a distinct religious and cultural identity for centuries.

Central to Yazidi belief is Melek Tawwus, commonly known as the Peacock Angel, who serves as the chief of seven holy beings responsible for carrying out God's will. Outsiders have frequently misunderstood this belief, with some falsely accusing the Yazidis of worshipping the devil because of superficial comparisons between Melek Tawwus and figures found in other religious traditions. Scholars and Yazidi religious leaders have repeatedly rejected this characterization, emphasizing that these misconceptions stem from misunderstandings of Yazidi theology rather than the religion itself. Nevertheless, these false accusations have contributed to centuries of discrimination and violence against the community.

Long before the rise of ISIS, the Yazidis experienced repeated persecution because of their distinct religious identity. Throughout history they faced forced conversions, displacement, and violent campaigns carried out by various rulers and extremist groups seeking religious conformity. Their relatively small population and geographic isolation further increased their vulnerability, making it difficult to defend their communities against organized attacks. This long history of marginalization created conditions that ISIS would later exploit, portraying the Yazidis as an illegitimate religious group whose existence threatened its vision of an Islamic caliphate.

Understanding this historical context demonstrates that the 2014 genocide did not occur in isolation. Rather, it represented the culmination of longstanding prejudice against the Yazidi people, amplified by ISIS's extremist ideology and its deliberate campaign to eradicate a religious minority from territory under its control.

ISIS, Religious Extremism, and the Process of Religious Racialization

The rise of the Islamic State of Iraq and Syria (ISIS) transformed longstanding prejudice against the Yazidi people into a coordinated campaign of mass violence. Emerging during periods of political instability in Iraq and Syria, ISIS sought to establish a self-declared caliphate governed by its extremist interpretation of Sunni Islam. The organization rejected religious pluralism and viewed communities that did not conform to its ideology as enemies. According to the RAND Corporation, ISIS claimed religious authority over all Muslims while advocating a violent ideology that justified persecution against those it considered unbelievers or apostates (RAND Corporation, 2022).

One concept that helps explain ISIS's treatment of the Yazidis is religious racialization. Religious racialization occurs when a religious community becomes perceived as a fixed and inferior group whose identity is treated as permanent rather than simply a matter of personal belief. Instead of recognizing religious affiliation as something individuals may choose or practice differently, perpetrators assign an entire population negative characteristics based solely on its religious identity. This process often strips individuals of their humanity and portrays them as fundamentally different or dangerous, making discrimination and violence easier to justify.

ISIS applied this process to the Yazidi community by portraying its members as illegitimate because of their religious beliefs. Extremist propaganda falsely characterized the Yazidi faith as devil worship, despite repeated explanations from scholars and Yazidi religious leaders that these accusations misrepresented their religion. Central to these misconceptions was the Yazidi reverence for Melek Tawwus, the Peacock Angel, whom ISIS falsely equated with the devil. As journalist Raya Jalabi explained, these longstanding misconceptions became especially dangerous during the rise of Islamic extremism, contributing to the perception that the Yazidis were religious outsiders who could be targeted without moral restraint (Jalabi, 2014).

Religious racialization also reflects broader patterns observed in many historical genocides. Perpetrators often construct rigid categories that separate one group from another, portraying the targeted population as inferior, impure, or threatening. Once these beliefs become accepted within extremist movements, violence can be reframed as a necessary act of purification or defense rather than recognized as a violation of fundamental human rights. In the case of ISIS, this ideology provided the justification for forced religious conversion, mass executions, sexual enslavement, and the systematic destruction of Yazidi communities.

Understanding religious racialization demonstrates that the Yazidi genocide was motivated by more than military conquest or territorial expansion. ISIS sought to eliminate a religious minority whose identity fundamentally conflicted with its extremist worldview. By examining the ideological foundations of this violence, scholars can better understand how prejudice evolves into organized persecution and why protecting religious minorities remains an essential component of international human rights efforts.

The Yazidi Genocide and Its Lasting Consequences

On August 3, 2014, ISIS launched a large-scale assault on the Sinjar region, home to one of the largest Yazidi populations in Iraq. The attack quickly overwhelmed local defenses, forcing tens of thousands of civilians to flee toward Mount Sinjar in search of safety. Many families became trapped on the mountain without adequate food, water, or medical care while ISIS militants surrounded the area. The humanitarian crisis drew widespread international attention as thousands of displaced Yazidis struggled to survive under extreme conditions.

As ISIS captured Yazidi communities, its campaign extended far beyond military occupation. Men and elderly boys were frequently separated from their families and executed if they refused to convert to ISIS's interpretation of Islam. Women and girls were systematically abducted and subjected to sexual slavery, forced marriage, and repeated physical and psychological abuse. Many children were also kidnapped, separated from their families, and, in some cases, forced into ISIS-controlled institutions where they were indoctrinated and trained for service within the organization. These actions reflected a deliberate strategy aimed not only at controlling territory but also at destroying the social fabric of the Yazidi community.

Beyond the widespread violence against individuals, ISIS also sought to eliminate Yazidi religious and cultural identity. Fighters destroyed shrines, temples, cemeteries, and other sites that held deep historical and spiritual significance. According to research by Benjamin Isakhan (2020), attacks on Yazidi heritage sites deprived the community of places essential to practicing religious traditions and preserving cultural memory. The destruction of these sites represented an effort to erase centuries of Yazidi history while weakening the community's ability to maintain its religious identity after displacement.

The consequences of the genocide continue to affect the Yazidi population today. Although ISIS no longer controls the territory it once occupied, thousands of Yazidis remain displaced, and many families continue searching for relatives who disappeared during the attacks. Survivors frequently face long-term physical injuries, psychological trauma, and economic hardship as they attempt to rebuild their lives. Entire communities were fragmented, and the recovery of cultural heritage, religious institutions, and community trust remains an ongoing challenge.

The international community has increasingly recognized the severity of these crimes. The United Nations and numerous governments have formally described ISIS's actions against the Yazidis as genocide, acknowledging that the organization's campaign was intended to destroy the group because of its religious identity. This recognition has strengthened efforts to document atrocities, support survivors, locate missing persons, and pursue accountability for those responsible. However, many human rights organizations argue that continued international cooperation will be necessary to ensure justice for survivors and to assist in the long-term recovery of Yazidi communities affected by the genocide.

Conclusion

The Yazidi genocide stands as one of the most severe examples of religious persecution in the twenty-first century and illustrates how extremist ideology can transform prejudice into systematic violence. Through the process of religious racialization, ISIS portrayed the Yazidi people as an illegitimate and inferior religious community, using these beliefs to justify mass killings, forced conversions, sexual slavery, kidnappings, and the destruction of cultural and religious heritage. These actions were not isolated acts of violence but components of a coordinated effort to eliminate an entire ethnoreligious group from territories under ISIS control.

Examining the Yazidi genocide demonstrates the devastating consequences that occur when religious identity becomes the basis for dehumanization. History has repeatedly shown that genocide is often preceded by the portrayal of targeted populations as fundamentally different, dangerous, or unworthy of protection. Understanding these warning signs allows scholars, policymakers, and human rights organizations to better recognize conditions that may lead to future mass atrocities and to develop strategies for prevention.

Although ISIS has largely lost its territorial control, the effects of the genocide continue to shape the lives of thousands of Yazidi survivors. Many remain displaced, families continue searching for missing relatives, and communities face the difficult task of rebuilding cultural institutions, religious traditions, and economic stability. International organizations have made progress in documenting crimes and supporting survivors, yet significant challenges remain in achieving justice and ensuring long-term recovery.

Ultimately, the Yazidi genocide demonstrates the importance of protecting vulnerable religious minorities and confronting ideologies that promote hatred, exclusion, and violence. Continued international cooperation, accountability for perpetrators, and sustained humanitarian support remain essential to preventing similar atrocities in the future. By studying the experiences of the Yazidi people, researchers and policymakers gain valuable insight into the mechanisms of religious persecution and the ongoing responsibility of the international community to defend fundamental human rights.
