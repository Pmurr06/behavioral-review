Rethinking Affordable Housing: A Land-Bank Strategy for Bozeman

Radek Janout

Finance student at Montana State University

Economics & Public Policy June 2026 9 min read

Keywords

Housing Affordability, Urban Development, Land Use, Public Policy, Montana, Affordable Housing, Zoning, Behavioral Economics

Introduction

Housing affordability has become one of the most urgent economic challenges facing the United States. Across the country, home prices and rents have risen faster than incomes, leaving many workers unable to live in the same communities where they are employed. This pressure is especially visible in fast-growing cities where population growth, limited housing supply, and rising land values have combined to make housing increasingly inaccessible.

Bozeman, Montana, offers a clear example of this problem. Once viewed as a smaller mountain city with a strong local identity, Bozeman has experienced rapid growth, rising demand, and increasing pressure on its housing market. For many residents, especially students, service workers, teachers, healthcare workers, and other essential employees, the cost of living has become difficult to sustain. The result is not only a housing issue, but a broader community issue. When workers are priced out of the places they serve, local businesses, schools, public services, and community institutions all feel the effects.

Much of the housing debate focuses on construction costs, rent levels, or the number of units being built. While those factors matter, they often overlook one of the most important barriers to affordability: the cost and scarcity of developable land. In cities like Bozeman, land is not simply a neutral input in the housing market. It is shaped by zoning, infrastructure access, investor expectations, population growth, and local political decisions. When developable land becomes scarce and expensive, the entire economics of housing production changes.

This article argues that Bozeman's housing affordability crisis requires a policy response focused directly on land. Rather than relying only on traditional affordable housing mandates or market-rate development, this proposal recommends creating a Mandatory Affordable Housing Land Requirement for significant new residential developments. Under this model, developers of larger residential projects would set aside a small portion of suitable land for affordable housing purposes, either by conveying land to the City of Bozeman or to an approved nonprofit housing partner.

The goal is not to stop development or punish builders. Instead, the goal is to ensure that new growth contributes to long-term housing accessibility. By creating a permanent affordable housing land bank, Bozeman could reduce one of the largest cost barriers in affordable housing production and give future policymakers, nonprofits, and housing providers a stronger foundation for addressing the crisis.

Bozeman and the Cost of Land

Bozeman's housing crisis did not emerge overnight. Over the past two decades, the city has experienced sustained population growth driven by Montana State University, a strong regional economy, outdoor recreation, and an increasing number of remote workers relocating from higher-cost metropolitan areas. Rising demand has brought economic opportunity, but it has also placed extraordinary pressure on the local housing market.

Nationally, housing affordability has steadily deteriorated. The ratio between the median home price and median household income has increased significantly over the past several decades, making homeownership increasingly difficult for middle-income Americans. Although this trend has affected much of the country, Bozeman has experienced particularly rapid appreciation due to its limited supply of developable land and continued population growth.

A major contributor to this shortage has been historical land-use policy. For many years, zoning regulations emphasized low-density residential development, limiting the construction of duplexes, townhomes, courtyard apartments, and other forms of what planners commonly describe as "missing middle" housing. While these policies were often intended to preserve neighborhood character, they also reduced the number of homes that could be built on valuable urban land. As demand increased, the limited supply of buildable lots became progressively more expensive.

Land prices matter because they represent one of the largest fixed costs in residential development. Construction materials, labor, financing, and permitting all contribute to housing costs, but developers must first acquire land before any building can begin. When land values rise substantially, developers naturally seek projects capable of generating the highest possible return on investment. In many markets, this encourages the construction of larger, higher-priced homes rather than moderately priced housing intended for working families.

Recent changes in Montana law have further complicated local housing policy. State legislation has limited some of the traditional tools municipalities previously used to require affordable housing as part of new residential development. As a result, local governments face growing pressure to identify new policy approaches that expand affordable housing opportunities while remaining consistent with state law.

These combined forces—rapid population growth, restrictive historical zoning, escalating land values, and changing legal authority—have produced a market where affordability becomes increasingly difficult to achieve through private development alone. The result is a housing market that functions efficiently for higher-income buyers while leaving many essential workers competing for a shrinking supply of attainable housing.

Recognizing that land itself has become the primary economic constraint shifts the policy conversation. Instead of asking only how affordable housing should be financed, policymakers must also consider how communities can preserve access to developable land before rising prices permanently eliminate future opportunities.

"Affordable housing policy should address not only the cost of construction, but also the scarcity of developable land."

The Policy Proposal

Addressing Bozeman's housing crisis requires more than increasing the number of homes built each year. It requires reducing the cost of one of housing's most expensive inputs: developable land. This proposal introduces a Mandatory Affordable Housing Land Requirement designed to create a permanent supply of land dedicated to future affordable housing development.

Under this policy, all new residential developments creating 20 or more housing units or residential lots would be required to permanently dedicate 5 percent of the project's gross developable land area to affordable housing purposes. Rather than constructing affordable units themselves, developers would convey this land to either the City of Bozeman or an approved nonprofit housing organization capable of managing future development.

To maximize its usefulness, the land would need to meet minimum development standards. Parcels should have access to public infrastructure, including water, sewer, and stormwater systems, be suitable for construction based on geotechnical conditions, and already possess zoning that allows relatively high residential density. By requiring developable land instead of isolated or unusable parcels, the policy would ensure that each contribution has meaningful long-term value.

The proposal differs from traditional inclusionary housing programs in an important way. Many affordable housing requirements obligate developers to construct a certain number of below-market-rate housing units within a project. While these programs can increase affordability, they also increase construction costs for individual developments and may discourage some projects from moving forward altogether. This proposal instead focuses on the long-term public asset: land.

Removing land acquisition costs from future affordable housing projects dramatically changes their financial feasibility. Nonprofit developers, housing authorities, and community land trusts frequently identify land acquisition as one of the greatest barriers to producing affordable housing. By establishing a permanent inventory of publicly controlled land, Bozeman could reduce future development costs while preserving flexibility over when and how affordable housing is ultimately constructed.

Recognizing that not every development site can reasonably accommodate a land dedication, the proposal also includes a Fee-in-Lieu alternative. If a physical land dedication is determined to be impractical because of site characteristics, infrastructure limitations, or project design, developers could instead contribute the appraised market value of the required land area into a dedicated Affordable Housing Land Acquisition Fund.

Unlike general municipal revenue, this fund would remain legally restricted for acquiring, preparing, and preserving land for future affordable housing development. Over time, both direct land dedications and fee contributions would gradually expand Bozeman's inventory of developable land, creating a long-term public resource capable of supporting affordable housing long after individual developments are completed.

Rather than viewing each new subdivision as an isolated project, this policy treats growth as an opportunity to gradually build a permanent housing asset for future generations. As Bozeman continues to expand, every major development would contribute, in some form, toward preserving housing opportunities for the workers, families, and students who sustain the city's economy.

Economic Rationale and Behavioral Incentives

At its core, housing is governed by the same economic principles as any other market: prices are determined by the interaction of supply and demand. When demand consistently outpaces supply, prices rise until fewer buyers are able to participate. While housing markets are influenced by many factors—including interest rates, construction costs, labor availability, and migration—land remains the one resource that cannot be manufactured. This makes it both economically valuable and highly susceptible to scarcity.

From a developer's perspective, rising land values create rational incentives. Purchasing expensive land requires generating sufficient revenue to justify the initial investment, financing costs, and development risk. As a result, developers frequently maximize returns by building larger or higher-priced homes that produce greater profit margins. This behavior is not necessarily driven by greed; rather, it reflects the economic incentives embedded within the housing market. When land costs increase, projects targeting middle-income households often become financially uncompetitive compared to higher-end developments.

Behavioral economics provides additional insight into why this pattern persists. Individuals and firms respond to incentives, particularly when those incentives involve significant financial risk. Developers are naturally inclined to pursue projects that maximize expected returns while minimizing uncertainty. Likewise, investors tend to allocate capital toward developments offering the greatest potential profitability. These decisions are individually rational, yet collectively they can produce market outcomes that fail to meet broader community needs.

Economists often describe this situation as a market failure. Although the housing market efficiently allocates resources according to profitability, it does not necessarily allocate housing according to social need. Essential workers—including teachers, healthcare professionals, first responders, service employees, and municipal staff—provide critical services that support local economies. However, their ability to compete in increasingly expensive housing markets is often limited by incomes that have not kept pace with rising property values. The result is a disconnect between what the market rewards and what communities require to function effectively.

The proposed land requirement seeks to correct this imbalance without eliminating market incentives altogether. Developers would continue earning returns on the vast majority of their projects while contributing a relatively small portion of land toward long-term public benefit. Rather than imposing permanent price controls or restricting future development, the policy introduces a predictable requirement that becomes another component of the development process.

Importantly, this proposal also attempts to align incentives rather than oppose them. Developers retain flexibility in project design, municipalities gradually expand their affordable housing capacity, and nonprofit housing organizations gain access to one of the most difficult resources to acquire independently. Over time, these incentives could encourage greater collaboration between private developers and public institutions rather than placing them in direct opposition.

Housing affordability ultimately reflects more than simple market dynamics; it reflects the incentives that shape individual decisions across governments, developers, investors, and consumers. If public policy hopes to produce different housing outcomes, it must first recognize that changing behavior often requires changing incentives. By targeting the cost of land rather than solely the price of housing, this proposal offers one possible framework for doing exactly that.

Legal and Practical Considerations

No housing policy is without trade-offs, and this proposal is no exception. Requiring developers to dedicate land or contribute an equivalent fee raises important legal, financial, and practical questions that policymakers would need to address before implementation.

One potential criticism is that additional development requirements could increase housing prices by raising the cost of building new homes. Developers may attempt to recover part of the land contribution through higher sale prices on the remaining units, particularly in markets where demand remains strong. Others argue that stricter requirements could discourage investment altogether, reducing the overall pace of residential construction.

These concerns deserve serious consideration. However, they should also be weighed against the long-term economic costs of continued housing inaccessibility. Communities that fail to provide attainable housing often experience labor shortages, longer employee commutes, declining workforce retention, and increasing pressure on public infrastructure. Businesses may struggle to recruit employees, while essential services become more difficult to staff. In this sense, the costs of inaction may ultimately exceed the costs associated with moderate development requirements.

Legal considerations also play an important role. Montana has enacted legislation limiting the authority of local governments to require certain forms of inclusionary housing. Because of these legal constraints, any land dedication policy would require careful review to ensure consistency with state law and constitutional property protections. Whether a land dedication requirement would withstand legal scrutiny would ultimately depend on its statutory design, proportionality, and judicial interpretation. As such, this proposal should be viewed as a framework for discussion rather than a definitive legal solution.

Implementation would likewise require clear administrative standards. Policymakers would need to define how developable land is appraised, establish objective criteria for determining when a Fee-in-Lieu payment is appropriate, and ensure that any dedicated land remains suitable for future residential development. Strong oversight would also be necessary to guarantee that land and financial contributions are used exclusively for affordable housing purposes rather than diverted to unrelated municipal expenses.

Despite these challenges, the proposal offers several practical advantages. Unlike one-time grant programs or temporary subsidy initiatives, a land bank grows over time. Each qualifying development gradually expands the community's inventory of land available for future affordable housing projects. This creates a long-term public asset that can support multiple generations of housing development rather than addressing only immediate shortages.

Ultimately, the success of any housing policy depends not only on economic theory but also on thoughtful implementation. By acknowledging legal constraints, anticipating unintended consequences, and maintaining flexibility through options such as Fee-in-Lieu payments, policymakers can improve the likelihood that innovative housing strategies produce meaningful and lasting results.

Conclusion

Housing affordability is often discussed as a problem of rising rents or insufficient housing supply, yet these symptoms frequently overlook one of the market's most fundamental constraints: the availability and cost of developable land. Bozeman's experience demonstrates how rapid population growth, restrictive land-use patterns, and escalating property values can combine to create a housing market that increasingly excludes the very workers who support the community's economy.

The proposal outlined in this article does not claim to solve the housing crisis on its own. Affordable housing is influenced by numerous factors, including interest rates, construction costs, infrastructure capacity, labor availability, and state housing policy. Nevertheless, reducing one of the largest fixed costs associated with affordable housing development offers a promising avenue for expanding future housing opportunities.

By requiring larger residential developments to contribute either developable land or an equivalent financial contribution toward future land acquisition, Bozeman could gradually establish a permanent inventory of land dedicated to affordable housing. Unlike temporary subsidies or one-time grant programs, a community land bank would continue growing alongside the city's expansion, creating opportunities for nonprofit organizations, housing authorities, and future policymakers to respond more effectively to changing housing needs.

Equally important, the proposal seeks to align economic incentives rather than replace them. Private development would remain the primary driver of housing construction, while new growth would also contribute to preserving long-term affordability. This balance recognizes that sustainable housing policy must support both economic development and community stability.

As cities throughout the United States continue searching for solutions to worsening housing affordability, policymakers should broaden the discussion beyond housing production alone. Addressing the underlying economics of land ownership may provide communities with an additional tool capable of expanding affordable housing opportunities without abandoning market-based development. While no single policy will eliminate the housing crisis, ensuring that future growth contributes to future affordability represents a meaningful step toward creating more resilient and economically inclusive communities.
