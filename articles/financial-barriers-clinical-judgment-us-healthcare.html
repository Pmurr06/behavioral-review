Financial Barriers and Clinical Judgment in the United States Healthcare System

Alexandra Quist

University of Washington

Public Policy July 2026 12 min read

Abstract

Financial constraints within the United States healthcare system frequently shape patient care and influence provider decision-making. Although clinicians strive to deliver patient-centered care, factors such as insurance policies, out-of-pocket expenses, and institutional requirements often override clinical judgment, resulting in delayed, altered, or deferred treatment. These barriers raise important concerns regarding health equity and access to care.

This qualitative study examines how financial barriers—including insurance limitations, medical costs, and healthcare system policies—affect clinical judgment and patients' ability to receive appropriate treatment. Seven semi-structured interviews were conducted with healthcare providers and patients who had experienced medical debt or cost-related barriers to care. Interview transcripts were analyzed using an inductive thematic approach to identify recurring patterns across participant experiences.

Three major themes emerged. First, patients frequently delayed, declined, or modified recommended treatment because of financial limitations. Second, providers routinely adapted care plans and navigated insurance requirements to secure coverage for medically appropriate treatment. Finally, financial considerations consistently influenced healthcare decisions, often taking precedence over providers' clinical judgment. These findings suggest that financial pressures function as structural determinants of care that undermine patient-centered decision-making and contribute to inequitable health outcomes. Addressing these systemic barriers is essential to improving both provider autonomy and patient access to high-quality healthcare within the United States.

Introduction

In 2021, an estimated 38.4 million Americans—approximately 12% of the U.S. population—were living with diabetes. Among adults younger than 65, roughly two million lacked health insurance, and in 2020 an estimated 1.3 million individuals reported rationing insulin because of its cost. Despite the prevalence of diabetes, treatment is highly individualized, as medication effectiveness and patient response can vary considerably between insulin formulations. Financial barriers can therefore have immediate and significant consequences for patient health.

During one of our interviews, a healthcare provider described caring for a patient whose diabetes had been well controlled using a brand-name insulin until their insurance provider unexpectedly changed its coverage policy. The new plan would only subsidize a generic alternative. Although generic medications are effective for many patients, this individual experienced poorer glucose control after the forced transition.

"They actually had a negative effect from that and they couldn't keep their numbers under control," Provider R explained.

The patient's healthcare team appealed the insurance company's decision and submitted clinical evidence supporting the continued use of the original medication. The appeal was denied, forcing both the provider and patient to abandon an effective treatment plan despite clear medical justification.

Experiences such as this illustrate a broader challenge within the United States healthcare system. Rather than being determined solely by clinical expertise and patient need, treatment decisions are frequently constrained by insurance policies, reimbursement requirements, and out-of-pocket costs. Although approximately 310 million Americans have some form of health insurance, coverage varies substantially across public and private plans. Many insured individuals remain underinsured, meaning that while they possess health insurance, their policies fail to provide adequate financial protection or meaningful access to recommended care. As a result, underinsured patients often encounter barriers similar to those faced by individuals without insurance.

For the nearly 27 million Americans who remain uninsured, these financial obstacles are even more pronounced. The United States consistently reports some of the highest healthcare and prescription drug costs among comparable high-income nations, due in part to the absence of comprehensive regulation of medical and pharmaceutical pricing. Consequently, patients and healthcare providers must routinely navigate financial considerations that can conflict with evidence-based medical decision-making.

This study investigates how these economic pressures shape both patient experiences and provider decision-making. Through qualitative interviews with healthcare providers and patients, we examine how insurance limitations, medical debt, and treatment costs influence clinical judgment and access to care. We argue that financial barriers have become structural determinants of healthcare delivery, limiting providers' ability to practice patient-centered medicine and contributing to inequitable health outcomes across the United States.

Methods

Study Design

This study employed a qualitative research design to examine how financial barriers within the United States healthcare system influence clinical decision-making and patient access to care. Semi-structured interviews were selected to capture detailed accounts of how insurance policies, medical debt, and healthcare costs shape treatment decisions from both patient and provider perspectives.

Participants and Data Collection

Seven semi-structured interviews were conducted during the fall of 2025 by a team of fifteen undergraduate researchers at the University of Washington. Interviews were conducted either in person or via Zoom to maximize accessibility. Participants included healthcare providers from a variety of professional backgrounds as well as patients who had personally experienced medical debt or cost-related barriers to healthcare.

Participants were recruited by individual student researchers, voluntarily agreed to participate, and received no financial compensation. Each participant provided verbal informed consent before and during the interview process and agreed to the recording, transcription, and research use of their responses. To protect confidentiality, all identifying information was removed during transcription and participants were assigned pseudonyms where appropriate. Interviews were designed to last approximately 45 minutes, although interview lengths varied slightly depending on participant responses.

Data Analysis

Interview transcripts were analyzed using an inductive thematic analysis. All fifteen student researchers collaboratively coded transcripts using Dedoose, a qualitative data analysis platform. Researchers independently identified meaningful excerpts and assigned preliminary codes before meeting in small groups to reconcile discrepancies, refine coding definitions, and establish consistency across the dataset.

Each transcript was reviewed by multiple researchers to improve reliability. Throughout the coding process, the research team engaged in regular reflexive discussions to examine positionality, identify potential sources of bias, and strengthen the consistency of thematic interpretation. These collaborative analyses produced several overarching themes describing how financial constraints influence clinical judgment, patient decision-making, and access to healthcare.

Ethical Considerations

All student researchers completed the University of Washington's required human subjects research training prior to data collection. The project received departmental approval, and all research procedures were conducted in accordance with institutional ethical standards for research involving human participants.

Results

Qualitative analysis of the interview transcripts identified three overarching themes describing how financial barriers influence healthcare decision-making. Across both patient and provider interviews, participants consistently described financial considerations as factors that altered recommended treatment, delayed care, and constrained providers' ability to rely solely on clinical judgment.

Patients Delay or Forgo Recommended Care

Participants frequently described postponing, modifying, or declining recommended medical treatment because of financial constraints. Although providers often attempted to recommend the most appropriate course of care, insurance limitations and out-of-pocket costs regularly prevented patients from pursuing those recommendations.

One provider described a Medicare patient who required dental implants but discovered that the procedure was not covered because it was not classified as medically necessary. As a result, the patient postponed treatment until they could afford the procedure independently.

"...the patient delayed implant surgery until they knew they had money to cover it." (Interview 1.25)

This experience reflects a broader pattern observed throughout the interviews. Financial barriers frequently became the deciding factor in whether patients received timely treatment, regardless of clinical recommendations. Participants described these economic pressures as immediate obstacles that often resulted in worsening health outcomes or prolonged suffering.

Providers Adapt Care to Navigate Financial Barriers

Healthcare providers consistently expressed a commitment to delivering the highest standard of patient care. However, participants described routinely modifying treatment plans to accommodate insurance requirements, coverage restrictions, and reimbursement policies rather than relying exclusively on clinical judgment.

One provider explained that appeals to insurance companies often became necessary when medically appropriate treatments were denied. Even when providers submitted supporting clinical evidence, approval was not guaranteed.

"...we had to completely change their care plan because certain medications, supplies, and suppliers were not covered by the patient's insurance." (Interview 2.25)

Several providers also described selecting diagnostic codes or treatment pathways that were more likely to receive insurance approval, even when those choices were shaped by administrative requirements rather than purely medical considerations. These accounts demonstrate how external financial pressures influence not only patient access to care but also the clinical decisions providers are able to implement.

Financial Barriers Override Clinical Judgment

The interviews revealed that financial limitations frequently superseded providers' medical recommendations. Patients often understood the care they required but acknowledged that cost made following those recommendations impossible.

"I do need to be periodically monitored... I just don't go because I can't afford it. Whenever I'm off government medical, I don't go to the doctor." (Interview 6.25)

This testimony illustrates the central finding of this study: clinical judgment alone does not determine healthcare delivery. Instead, financial accessibility frequently dictates whether evidence-based treatment can be pursued. Across both provider and patient interviews, economic constraints repeatedly emerged as structural barriers that prevented patient-centered care, often forcing providers and patients alike to compromise on medically preferred treatment options.

Discussion

The findings of this study demonstrate that financial realities significantly influence both patient care and clinical decision-making within the United States healthcare system. Although healthcare providers consistently expressed a commitment to delivering patient-centered care, their ability to implement evidence-based treatment plans was frequently constrained by insurance policies, medication costs, and institutional financial requirements. Rather than serving as secondary considerations, these economic factors often became primary determinants of the care patients ultimately received.

Patient interviews revealed that financial barriers routinely influenced healthcare decisions. Participants described delaying treatment, postponing prescription refills, rationing medication, or avoiding medical care altogether because of cost. These findings are consistent with previous research on financial toxicity, which describes the economic burden of healthcare as a significant contributor to poorer health outcomes and reduced treatment adherence (Zafar & Abernethy). Our interviews illustrate that these consequences extend beyond financial hardship alone, affecting patients' ability to receive timely, evidence-based medical care.

The financial pressures experienced by patients also created substantial challenges for healthcare providers. Participants consistently described situations in which insurance restrictions overruled their clinical judgment, requiring them to modify treatment plans, submit repeated insurance appeals, or navigate administrative requirements to secure medically appropriate care. These experiences closely mirror existing literature describing the growing influence of corporate and payer-driven decision-making within clinical practice (Hunt et al., 2019). Rather than exercising complete clinical autonomy, providers frequently found themselves balancing medical expertise with financial and bureaucratic constraints.

Several providers described strategically navigating insurance systems to improve patients' access to treatment, including selecting diagnostic codes or pursuing alternative approval pathways when standard recommendations were denied. While these practices reflected providers' commitment to patient advocacy, they also highlighted the extent to which administrative systems shape clinical decision-making. In one interview, a provider described being unable to dispense necessary medication to a patient traveling from Alaska because the patient could not afford the prescription. Experiences such as these demonstrate how financial barriers can prevent providers from delivering the standard of care they believe is medically appropriate.

This study has several limitations. The sample consisted of only seven interviews conducted within a limited geographic region, restricting the generalizability of the findings. In addition, participant recruitment occurred over a single academic quarter, limiting the diversity of healthcare settings and patient experiences represented. Nevertheless, the consistency of themes across both provider and patient interviews suggests that financial barriers remain a persistent influence on healthcare delivery.

Future research should examine these dynamics across larger and more geographically diverse populations while exploring differences among medical specialties, healthcare systems, and insurance models. Understanding how financial pressures shape clinical decision-making across a broader range of settings may help identify policy reforms that better support both healthcare providers and the patients they serve.

Conclusion

Healthcare providers strive to deliver patient-centered care guided by clinical expertise and evidence-based practice. However, the findings of this study demonstrate that financial constraints frequently override clinical judgment, limiting both providers' ability to recommend optimal treatment and patients' ability to receive it. Insurance restrictions, high out-of-pocket costs, and broader economic pressures consistently emerged as structural barriers that shaped healthcare decisions across both patient and provider interviews.

Participants described delaying treatment, modifying care plans, appealing insurance denials, and, in some cases, forgoing necessary medical care altogether because of financial limitations. These experiences illustrate that healthcare decisions within the United States are often influenced as much by economic realities as by medical necessity. Consequently, providers are frequently forced to balance sound clinical judgment with administrative and financial constraints beyond their control.

Although this study is limited by its sample size and geographic scope, the consistency of findings across interviews underscores the pervasive influence of financial barriers on healthcare delivery. Future research should investigate how these pressures vary across medical specialties, healthcare systems, and patient populations to better understand where targeted policy reforms may have the greatest impact.

Ultimately, improving patient-centered care requires more than advances in medicine alone. Reducing financial barriers that prevent patients from accessing appropriate treatment is essential to strengthening provider autonomy, promoting health equity, and ensuring that clinical decisions are guided foremost by patient need rather than economic constraint.
