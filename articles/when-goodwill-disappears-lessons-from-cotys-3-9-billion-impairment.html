When Goodwill Disappears: Lessons from Coty's $3.9 Billion Impairment

Luke Marshall

Finance
University of Oregon

Finance Case Study July 2026 12 min read

Abstract

This finance case study examines Coty Inc.'s 2019 goodwill impairment following its acquisition of Procter & Gamble's beauty brands. It explains how goodwill is recognized under U.S. GAAP, why the impairment occurred, how management estimated fair value, and why impairment charges provide meaningful information for investors evaluating acquisition performance and corporate financial health.

Keywords

Finance, Accounting, Corporate Finance, Business & Economics, Financial Reporting, Goodwill, Goodwill Impairment, Mergers & Acquisitions, SEC Filings, Financial Statement Analysis, Corporate Valuation, Investor Analysis

Introduction

Corporate acquisitions are often viewed as opportunities to accelerate growth, expand market share, and strengthen competitive advantage. However, acquisitions also expose companies to significant financial risk when the value of acquired assets fails to meet expectations. One of the clearest indicators of this risk is a goodwill impairment, which occurs when the value of an acquired business declines below the amount originally recorded on a company's balance sheet. Because goodwill often represents billions of dollars in acquisition value, impairment charges can substantially affect a company's reported earnings and influence investor confidence.

A notable example occurred in 2019 when Coty Inc., a global beauty company, recorded a $3.85 billion goodwill impairment charge following continued weakness in its Consumer Beauty business. The impairment was largely attributed to declining market performance, changing consumer preferences, and lower-than-expected financial results following Coty's acquisition of Procter & Gamble's beauty brands. Although the impairment produced a significant accounting loss, it also highlighted the challenges companies face when integrating major acquisitions and estimating the future value of acquired assets.

Goodwill impairment extends beyond a single accounting adjustment. It reflects management's reassessment of whether an acquisition is expected to generate the economic benefits originally anticipated. As a result, impairment charges provide investors with valuable insight into acquisition performance, management decision-making, and the long-term value of a company's strategic investments.

This case study examines Coty's 2019 goodwill impairment by explaining how goodwill is recognized under U.S. Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP), why the impairment occurred, how the company determined the amount of the write-down, and what lessons investors and financial analysts can draw from one of the largest impairment charges in Coty's history.

Understanding Goodwill

Goodwill is an intangible asset created when a company acquires another business for a purchase price that exceeds the fair value of its identifiable net assets. Rather than representing a physical asset, goodwill reflects the value of intangible benefits such as brand recognition, customer relationships, intellectual property, workforce expertise, and anticipated future earnings. Because these advantages cannot always be separately identified or measured, they are recorded collectively as goodwill on the acquiring company's balance sheet.

Unlike most long-term assets, goodwill is not amortized under U.S. Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP). Instead, companies are required to evaluate goodwill for impairment at least annually, or more frequently if events or changing business conditions suggest that its recorded value may no longer be recoverable. This accounting approach recognizes that goodwill does not necessarily decline in value on a predictable schedule but may experience sudden reductions if an acquisition fails to perform as expected.

Coty follows this accounting policy by recording goodwill when the purchase price of an acquired business exceeds the fair value of its identifiable net assets. The company performs annual impairment testing at the reporting-unit level, which during fiscal year 2019 consisted primarily of its Luxury, Consumer Beauty, Professional Beauty, and Corporate reporting units. If the estimated fair value of a reporting unit falls below its carrying value, the company recognizes an impairment charge equal to the amount by which goodwill has become overstated.

Because goodwill often represents a significant portion of the purchase price in large acquisitions, impairment testing plays a critical role in ensuring that financial statements continue to reflect a company's economic reality. When impairment occurs, it signals that the future benefits originally expected from an acquisition have diminished, providing investors with an important indicator of changes in business performance and acquisition success.

Coty's 2019 Goodwill Impairment

Coty's 2019 goodwill impairment stemmed largely from the underperformance of its Consumer Beauty reporting unit, which had experienced declining financial results following the company's acquisition of Procter & Gamble's beauty brands. While the acquisition significantly expanded Coty's portfolio of cosmetics, fragrances, and hair care products, the company faced ongoing operational challenges that prevented the acquired assets from generating the level of profitability originally anticipated.

During fiscal year 2019, changing consumer preferences, increased competition, supply chain disruptions, and continued losses in market share negatively affected the Consumer Beauty segment's projected future cash flows. As management reassessed the long-term financial outlook of the reporting unit, it concluded that the carrying value of the business exceeded its estimated fair value, requiring a substantial impairment charge under U.S. GAAP.

As a result, Coty recorded a goodwill impairment charge of approximately $3.85 billion during fiscal year 2019. The impairment represented one of the largest accounting charges in the company's history and significantly reduced reported earnings for the year. Although the write-down did not directly affect the company's cash flows, it reflected management's recognition that the economic value of the acquired business had declined since the acquisition.

Without recording the impairment, Coty's pre-tax income would have been approximately $380.4 million, compared to $153.3 million in the previous fiscal year. While the impairment dramatically changed the company's reported earnings, the adjusted results illustrate that Coty's underlying operating performance remained profitable before recognizing the non-cash accounting charge. At the same time, the impairment served as an important signal that management's original expectations for the acquisition had not been fully realized, highlighting the long-term financial risks associated with large corporate acquisitions.

How Coty Measured the Impairment

To determine whether goodwill remained recoverable, Coty performed its required annual impairment assessment in accordance with U.S. GAAP. The objective of the test was to determine whether the fair value of each reporting unit exceeded its carrying value. If a reporting unit's carrying value was greater than its estimated fair value, the company was required to recognize a goodwill impairment charge equal to the amount of the excess, limited to the recorded goodwill balance.

Coty first had the option to perform a qualitative assessment to evaluate whether it was more likely than not that a reporting unit's fair value was less than its carrying value. If qualitative factors suggested potential impairment, or if management elected to bypass the qualitative assessment, the company performed a quantitative impairment test.

The quantitative analysis relied on two widely accepted valuation approaches. Under the income approach, Coty estimated the present value of each reporting unit's expected future cash flows by discounting projected earnings using a rate that reflected the risks associated with the business. Under the market approach, the company estimated value by comparing each reporting unit with similar publicly traded companies and market transactions within the beauty and consumer products industry.

After applying these valuation methods, Coty determined that the estimated fair value of its Consumer Beauty reporting unit had fallen below its carrying value. The decline reflected lower expected future earnings and reduced long-term growth expectations resulting from continued operational challenges. Consequently, the company recognized a goodwill impairment charge of approximately $3.85 billion, with the vast majority of the write-down attributable to the Consumer Beauty reporting unit.

By combining both discounted cash flow analysis and market-based valuation techniques, Coty sought to develop a reasonable estimate of fair value that incorporated both company-specific financial expectations and broader market conditions. Although impairment testing requires significant management judgment, these valuation methods are widely used because they provide a comprehensive framework for assessing whether goodwill continues to represent future economic benefits.

Key Assumptions and Management Estimates

Estimating the fair value of a reporting unit requires significant management judgment because it depends on assumptions about future business performance rather than directly observable market prices. As a result, even relatively small changes in these assumptions can materially affect whether a company records an impairment charge and the amount recognized.

For Coty's 2019 impairment analysis, management identified projected future cash flows as one of the most significant components of its valuation models. These projections incorporated assumptions regarding future revenue growth, operating performance, profitability, and long-term market conditions within the beauty industry. Due to continued weakness in the Consumer Beauty segment, the company incorporated a projected long-term revenue growth rate of approximately -5.9%, reflecting expectations of declining business performance.

Another critical assumption was the discount rate, which Coty estimated at approximately 8%. The discount rate represents the required rate of return investors would expect for assuming the risks associated with the reporting unit. Because future cash flows are discounted back to their present value, even modest changes in the discount rate can substantially affect estimated fair value.

Coty's sensitivity analysis demonstrated the importance of these assumptions. Management disclosed that a 1% further decline in projected revenue growth would have resulted in an additional impairment charge of approximately $214 million. Likewise, a 0.5% increase in the discount rate would have reduced the estimated fair value sufficiently to require approximately $195.5 million in additional impairment. These disclosures illustrate how sensitive goodwill valuations are to relatively small changes in management's expectations.

Because impairment testing relies heavily on forecasts rather than historical performance alone, goodwill valuations involve considerable estimation uncertainty. Investors should therefore view impairment analyses not as precise calculations, but as informed estimates based on management's best assessment of future operating conditions. Understanding these assumptions provides important context when evaluating the financial health of companies that have grown through significant acquisitions.

Why Goodwill Impairment Matters to Investors

Although goodwill impairment is a non-cash accounting charge, it often carries significant implications for investors, creditors, and financial analysts. Large impairment charges can indicate that an acquisition has not generated the financial benefits management originally expected, raising questions about capital allocation decisions and the company's long-term growth strategy. As a result, impairment announcements frequently receive substantial attention from financial markets and the business media.

Coty's 2019 impairment illustrates how changing market conditions can alter the value of an acquisition years after a transaction is completed. Following the acquisition of Procter & Gamble's beauty brands, the company faced declining sales, increased competition, operational inefficiencies, and evolving consumer preferences that reduced the expected profitability of its Consumer Beauty business. These factors ultimately required management to reassess the value of the acquired assets and recognize a substantial write-down.

Media coverage of the impairment emphasized the broader challenges facing the company. Reuters reported that Coty's restructuring efforts and multi-billion-dollar asset write-down reflected continued weakness in its consumer beauty portfolio following the Procter & Gamble acquisition. Investors reacted negatively to the announcement, viewing the impairment as evidence that the acquisition had not delivered the anticipated financial performance despite its strategic objectives.

From an investment perspective, goodwill impairment should not be evaluated solely as an accounting loss. Instead, it should be considered alongside a company's operating performance, cash flow generation, competitive position, and management's plans to improve future profitability. While impairment charges reduce reported earnings, they can also provide investors with valuable information about management's updated expectations for the business and the long-term success of previous acquisition decisions.

Conclusion

Coty's 2019 goodwill impairment demonstrates the financial risks that can accompany large corporate acquisitions and the importance of accurately valuing acquired businesses over time. Although the company initially expected significant long-term benefits from its acquisition of Procter & Gamble's beauty brands, declining operating performance within the Consumer Beauty segment ultimately reduced the value of those expected future economic benefits. As a result, Coty recognized a goodwill impairment charge of approximately $3.85 billion, one of the largest accounting write-downs in the company's history.

The case also illustrates the complexity of goodwill impairment testing. Management relied on discounted cash flow models, market-based valuation techniques, projected revenue growth, and discount rate assumptions to estimate the fair value of its reporting units. Because these estimates require considerable professional judgment, even relatively small changes in assumptions can materially affect reported financial results and lead to additional impairment charges.

For investors, Coty's impairment serves as a reminder that acquisition success cannot be measured solely by the size or strategic rationale of a transaction. Instead, the long-term value of an acquisition depends on a company's ability to integrate acquired businesses, maintain competitive performance, and generate the future cash flows that justified the original purchase price. Goodwill impairments therefore provide valuable insight into management's reassessment of acquisition performance and can serve as an important indicator of changing business conditions.

Ultimately, the Coty case highlights why understanding goodwill and impairment accounting is essential for finance professionals, investors, and financial statement users. While impairment charges are non-cash in nature, they often reveal meaningful information about corporate strategy, management expectations, and the economic reality underlying reported financial statements.
