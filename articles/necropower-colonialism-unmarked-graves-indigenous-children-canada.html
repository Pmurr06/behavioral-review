Beyond Death: Necropower, Colonialism, and the Unmarked Graves of Indigenous Children in Canada

Alexandra Quist

Anthropology, University of Washington

Sociology & Anthropology July 2026 9 min read

Introduction

The discovery of hundreds of unmarked graves at former residential schools across Canada has forced the nation to confront one of the most devastating legacies of its colonial history. These burial sites, containing the remains of Indigenous children who died while attending government- and church-operated residential schools, stand as powerful evidence of systemic violence, cultural suppression, and institutional neglect. Although public attention has largely focused on the recent discoveries themselves, the graves represent only one dimension of a much broader system of colonial oppression that sought to erase Indigenous identities, communities, and ways of life.

Canada's residential school system was established with the explicit goal of assimilating Indigenous children into Euro-Canadian society by separating them from their families, languages, and cultural traditions. Far from functioning as ordinary boarding schools, these institutions subjected many children to physical abuse, emotional trauma, neglect, disease, and death. The unmarked graves left behind are therefore not merely historical artifacts; they symbolize a deliberate system in which Indigenous lives were devalued both in life and after death.

This article examines the significance of these burial sites through several anthropological concepts, including colonialism, necropower, necroviolence, temporalities of death and dying, and practices of dehumanization. Together, these perspectives reveal that the unmarked graves are more than physical locations where children were buried—they are enduring symbols of cultural erasure, structural violence, and the continuing struggle for truth, remembrance, and justice.

Necropower and Colonial Erasure

Colonialism extends far beyond the occupation of land. It also seeks to control identity, memory, and, ultimately, death itself. Within this framework, the concept of necropower describes the authority to determine not only who lives and who dies, but also whose lives are remembered, whose deaths are acknowledged, and whose histories are erased. Canada's residential school system exemplified this form of power by attempting to eliminate Indigenous cultures through the forced assimilation of Indigenous children.

Chief Bobby Cameron summarized this reality by stating, "The only crime we ever committed as children was being born Indigenous." This statement reflects the underlying ideology that justified the residential school system: Indigenous identity itself was treated as something to be suppressed rather than preserved. Children were prohibited from speaking their own languages, practicing their cultural traditions, or maintaining meaningful connections with their families and communities (Austen & Bilefsky, 2021). These policies were not isolated acts of discrimination but components of a broader colonial project aimed at dismantling Indigenous cultures across generations.

The unmarked graves discovered at former residential school sites demonstrate that this exercise of colonial power extended beyond life itself. Many children who died while attending these institutions were buried anonymously, often without informing their families or allowing culturally meaningful burial practices. In death, as in life, these children were denied recognition, dignity, and remembrance. The absence of names, ceremonies, and official records transformed individual tragedies into collective acts of erasure, reinforcing the colonial authority to determine not only the conditions of life but also the legacy of death.

Viewed through the lens of necropower, these burial sites represent more than evidence of historical neglect. They reveal how colonial institutions exercised authority over Indigenous existence at every stage of life, extending their influence into memory itself. The recent efforts to identify these burial sites and acknowledge the children they contain therefore challenge decades of institutional silence and begin restoring histories that colonial systems attempted to erase.

Necroviolence Beyond Death

The colonial exercise of necropower did not end with the deaths of Indigenous children. It extended into what anthropologists describe as necroviolence—the continuation of violence through the treatment, neglect, or erasure of human remains after death. Rather than ending suffering, death became another stage through which colonial institutions denied Indigenous children dignity, identity, and cultural belonging.

The unmarked graves discovered at former residential schools illustrate this continuing form of violence. Many children were buried without informing their families, recording their identities, or allowing culturally significant funerary practices to take place. In doing so, residential school authorities denied both the deceased and their communities the opportunity to mourn according to their traditions. The absence of names, memorials, and documented burials transformed these children from individuals into anonymous casualties of a broader colonial system.

Necroviolence extends beyond the moment of burial. Its effects continue whenever the identities of the deceased remain unknown, their histories are overlooked, or their communities are denied acknowledgment and accountability. For many Indigenous families, the recent discoveries of unmarked graves have reopened wounds that were never permitted to heal because the truth surrounding these deaths had been concealed for generations.

The continued investigation of former residential school sites therefore represents more than an archaeological effort. Identifying burial locations, recovering names where possible, and acknowledging the circumstances surrounding these deaths challenge decades of institutional silence. These efforts interrupt the ongoing effects of necroviolence by restoring recognition to children whose identities and humanity were deliberately obscured. Although many questions remain unanswered, every recovered story represents an important step toward historical accountability and collective remembrance.

Temporalities of Death and Disrupted Grief

The concept of temporalities of death and dying examines how death is experienced across time, particularly when mourning, remembrance, and closure are interrupted or denied. In the case of Canada's residential schools, the concealment of children's deaths fundamentally altered the grieving process for Indigenous families and communities. Because many deaths were never acknowledged and burial locations remained unknown for decades, grief was suspended rather than resolved, becoming an enduring source of intergenerational trauma.

The recent discovery of unmarked graves has therefore reshaped not only historical understanding but also the relationship between past and present. Families who were denied answers for generations are now confronting losses that were intentionally obscured by colonial institutions. These discoveries illustrate that the consequences of colonial violence are not confined to the past; they continue to influence contemporary communities through unresolved grief, disrupted family histories, and ongoing demands for truth and accountability.

This disruption is both emotional and political. As burial sites are investigated and new evidence emerges, governments, churches, and institutions are increasingly required to confront historical actions that were once ignored or concealed. Archaeologists, including Kisha Supernant, have worked alongside Indigenous communities to investigate former residential school sites with the goal of identifying burial locations while respecting cultural protocols and community leadership (Lebans, 2022). These collaborative efforts seek not only to recover physical evidence but also to restore historical truth to families who were denied the opportunity to mourn.

Viewed through the lens of temporalities of death and dying, these investigations represent more than acts of historical research. They interrupt decades of institutional silence and allow grief that was forcibly suspended to begin moving toward acknowledgment and remembrance. Although no investigation can reverse the suffering experienced by these children and their communities, recognizing their deaths and restoring their stories are essential steps toward justice, healing, and historical accountability.

Dehumanization in Life and Death

Underlying the systems of colonialism, necropower, and necroviolence was a broader process of dehumanization. Dehumanization occurs when individuals or groups are denied their inherent dignity, identity, and rights, making violence and discrimination appear justified or acceptable. Within Canada's residential school system, Indigenous children were not treated as individuals with distinct cultures, families, and identities. Instead, they were viewed as obstacles to colonial assimilation, reducing their humanity to a perceived problem that required correction.

This dehumanization shaped both the lives and deaths of Indigenous children. While attending residential schools, many experienced physical abuse, emotional trauma, neglect, and chronic malnutrition. Poor living conditions weakened immune systems and contributed to the spread of diseases such as tuberculosis, which claimed the lives of many children within the school system (Mosby & Millions, 2021). These deaths were not isolated tragedies but the foreseeable consequences of institutions that consistently failed to protect the children entrusted to their care.

The process of dehumanization did not end after death. Many children were buried without names, culturally appropriate funerary practices, or meaningful acknowledgment of their lives. By denying families the opportunity to recover, identify, or properly mourn their loved ones, residential school authorities extended the erasure that had begun during the children's lives. The absence of memorialization reinforced the colonial message that Indigenous lives—and by extension, Indigenous deaths—were considered less worthy of recognition and remembrance.

The ongoing efforts to identify burial sites, commemorate those who died, and return knowledge to Indigenous communities directly challenge this legacy of dehumanization. Restoring names where possible, acknowledging historical responsibility, and recognizing the humanity of the children who were lost affirm that every life carries inherent dignity. These acts of remembrance do not erase the violence of the past, but they represent an important step toward restoring the identities that colonial institutions sought to eliminate.

Conclusion

The discovery of unmarked graves at former residential schools represents far more than a historical revelation. These burial sites expose the enduring consequences of a colonial system that sought to erase Indigenous identities through assimilation, neglect, and institutional violence. Viewed through the anthropological concepts of necropower, necroviolence, temporalities of death and dying, and dehumanization, the graves emerge not only as places of loss but also as enduring symbols of cultural erasure and structural oppression.

The children buried within these sites were denied far more than life itself. Many were separated from their families, stripped of their languages and cultural identities, and ultimately deprived of the dignity of recognition in death. The concealment of their burial places prolonged grief across generations, transforming individual tragedies into a collective legacy of unresolved loss. Recovering these histories is therefore not simply an effort to document the past but a necessary step toward restoring identities that colonial institutions attempted to erase.

At the same time, the continuing investigation of former residential school sites demonstrates the resilience of Indigenous communities and their ongoing pursuit of truth, remembrance, and accountability. Archaeological research, historical documentation, and community-led initiatives have become powerful acts of remembrance that challenge decades of institutional silence while honoring the lives of children whose stories were deliberately obscured.

Ultimately, the significance of these graves extends beyond Canada alone. They illustrate how systems of colonial power can continue to shape memory, grief, and historical narratives long after violence has ended. Recognizing these children, acknowledging the injustices they endured, and supporting Indigenous communities in their pursuit of truth are essential not only for historical accountability but also for understanding how societies confront the lasting consequences of colonial violence. Remembering those who were once intentionally forgotten affirms a fundamental principle: every life deserves dignity, every death deserves recognition, and no people should have their history erased.
