The Rule of Reason: Why NCAA v. Alston Changed College Sports

Carson Wais

Business Finance student at Washington State University

Law & Criminal Justice June 2026 9 min read

Keywords

NCAA, Antitrust Law, Sherman Act, Rule of Reason, Supreme Court, College Athletics, Business Law, Amateurism

Introduction

For decades, the National Collegiate Athletic Association (NCAA) defended the principle of amateurism as the foundation of college athletics. Student-athletes generated billions of dollars in television contracts, ticket sales, sponsorships, and merchandise while remaining subject to strict limits on the educational benefits and compensation they could receive. The NCAA argued that these restrictions distinguished collegiate athletics from professional sports and preserved consumer interest in amateur competition.

That model faced its greatest legal challenge in National Collegiate Athletic Association v. Alston (2021). Rather than asking whether student-athletes should become professional employees, the Supreme Court considered a narrower but highly significant question: could the NCAA lawfully restrict education-related benefits under federal antitrust law? The answer would reshape the legal relationship between universities, athletic conferences, and the athletes who make collegiate sports possible.

At the center of the dispute was the Sherman Antitrust Act, a federal statute designed to promote competition and prevent organizations from imposing unreasonable restraints on trade. Former and current Division I student-athletes argued that the NCAA's restrictions artificially limited competition among schools by preventing universities from offering additional education-related benefits to recruit athletes. The NCAA responded that such restrictions were necessary to preserve the amateur nature of college sports and maintain public demand for its athletic competitions.

In a unanimous decision, the Supreme Court rejected the NCAA's argument and affirmed that the organization remained subject to the same antitrust principles that govern other industries. Applying the long-established "Rule of Reason" analysis, the Court concluded that the NCAA failed to demonstrate why its restrictions on education-related benefits were necessary to achieve its stated objectives.

This article examines the legal reasoning behind NCAA v. Alston, explains why the Rule of Reason proved decisive, and explores how the decision has influenced the evolving legal and financial landscape of collegiate athletics. Although the ruling addressed only education-related benefits, its broader implications continue to shape debates surrounding athlete compensation, institutional authority, and competition within one of America's largest sporting organizations.

The NCAA's Amateurism Model

Since its founding in 1906, the National Collegiate Athletic Association has governed intercollegiate athletics by establishing rules intended to preserve amateur competition. Central to this mission was the belief that student-athletes should participate primarily for educational and personal development rather than financial gain. Although universities could provide scholarships and certain educational benefits, the NCAA maintained strict limitations on additional forms of compensation in order to distinguish collegiate athletics from professional sports.

For many years, this model faced relatively little legal opposition. However, as college athletics evolved into a multibillion-dollar industry, criticism of the NCAA's compensation policies steadily increased. Television broadcasting rights, corporate sponsorships, merchandise sales, and postseason tournaments generated enormous revenues for universities and athletic conferences, while many student-athletes remained unable to receive benefits beyond those explicitly authorized by NCAA regulations. Critics argued that this imbalance created a system in which the individuals responsible for generating substantial economic value possessed limited bargaining power.

The legal challenge that ultimately reached the Supreme Court focused on education-related benefits rather than direct salaries. Current and former Division I student-athletes argued that NCAA rules unlawfully prevented universities from competing by offering additional academic incentives such as graduate scholarships, postgraduate internships, educational equipment, computers, tutoring, or other benefits connected to a student's education. According to the plaintiffs, these restrictions reduced competition among schools and violated federal antitrust law by artificially limiting what universities could offer prospective athletes.

The NCAA defended its position by arguing that these restrictions were necessary to preserve amateurism, which it claimed distinguished college athletics from professional leagues and maintained consumer demand. According to the organization, allowing schools to compete through expanded educational benefits would gradually erode the distinction between amateur and professional sports, ultimately reducing public interest in collegiate competition.

Although preserving amateur competition represented a legitimate organizational objective, the legal question before the courts was not whether amateurism possessed value. Instead, the issue became whether the NCAA's restrictions on education-related benefits unlawfully restrained competition under the Sherman Antitrust Act. Resolving that question required courts to examine not only the NCAA's stated objectives but also whether less restrictive alternatives existed that could achieve similar goals without unnecessarily limiting competition between member institutions.

By the time the dispute reached the Supreme Court, the case had grown beyond a disagreement over scholarships. It had become a broader examination of how much regulatory authority a private athletic organization could exercise when its rules affected competition within a significant national market. The Court's answer would reshape the legal framework governing college athletics and establish important limits on the NCAA's ability to justify restrictive policies under federal antitrust law.

The Rule of Reason and the Supreme Court

The Supreme Court's decision in NCAA v. Alston ultimately turned on one of the most established principles in American antitrust law: the Rule of Reason. Rather than declaring every restraint on competition automatically illegal, this legal framework requires courts to examine whether a challenged restriction produces unreasonable anticompetitive effects when balanced against any legitimate business justifications. The Rule of Reason recognizes that some restraints may be necessary for organizations to function effectively, but those restraints must remain proportional and reasonably connected to legitimate objectives.

Throughout the litigation, the NCAA argued that limiting education-related benefits was necessary to preserve the amateur character of college athletics. According to the organization, consumers valued college sports because athletes competed as students rather than professionals. The NCAA maintained that expanding permissible benefits could gradually blur the distinction between collegiate and professional athletics, ultimately reducing public interest and harming the product itself.

The courts acknowledged that preserving amateurism represented a legitimate objective. However, the existence of a legitimate goal alone was not sufficient to satisfy antitrust law. Under the Rule of Reason, the NCAA also needed to demonstrate that its restrictions were reasonably necessary to achieve that objective and that less restrictive alternatives would not accomplish the same purpose. After reviewing extensive evidence presented throughout the litigation, both the lower courts and the Supreme Court concluded that the NCAA failed to meet this burden.

The Court emphasized that education-related benefits differed significantly from unrestricted compensation. Allowing universities to provide additional academic resources, postgraduate scholarships, educational technology, or tutoring services did not necessarily transform student-athletes into professional employees. Instead, these benefits remained closely connected to the educational mission that the NCAA itself claimed to promote. As a result, prohibiting universities from competing through educational benefits represented a greater restraint on competition than was reasonably necessary.

Justice Neil Gorsuch, writing for a unanimous Court, affirmed that the NCAA was not exempt from the Sherman Antitrust Act simply because it governed college athletics. Like other organizations participating in interstate commerce, the NCAA remained subject to federal competition law. Justice Brett Kavanaugh's separate concurring opinion went even further, suggesting that many of the NCAA's broader compensation restrictions could face significant legal challenges in future litigation if subjected to similar antitrust scrutiny.

The decision therefore represented more than a dispute over educational benefits. It reaffirmed a fundamental principle of American competition law: organizations cannot avoid antitrust review merely by asserting that their industry is unique. Even institutions with longstanding traditions must demonstrate that restrictions on competition are legally justified rather than simply historically accepted. In doing so, NCAA v. Alston established an important precedent not only for collegiate athletics but also for how courts evaluate competitive restraints imposed by powerful private organizations.

Why the Decision Matters

Although NCAA v. Alston addressed only education-related benefits, its significance extends well beyond the specific issue presented before the Court. The ruling marked one of the strongest judicial challenges to the NCAA's traditional governance model and signaled that longstanding organizational practices would no longer receive automatic legal deference simply because they had existed for decades. Instead, the NCAA would be required to justify its restrictions under the same antitrust principles applied to other industries.

One of the decision's most important implications concerns competition among universities. Before Alston, NCAA regulations prevented member institutions from competing through expanded education-related benefits, effectively establishing a uniform ceiling on what schools could offer prospective student-athletes. Following the Court's decision, universities gained greater flexibility to provide additional educational opportunities, encouraging competition that may ultimately benefit student-athletes while remaining consistent with the educational mission of higher education.

The decision also reflects a broader shift in how courts and the public view college athletics. For many years, the NCAA successfully argued that amateurism justified restrictions that would likely be unlawful in other markets. However, as collegiate athletics evolved into a multibillion-dollar enterprise supported by lucrative media contracts, sponsorship agreements, and conference revenues, increasing attention was directed toward whether student-athletes were receiving a fair share of the economic value they helped create. Alston did not answer every question regarding athlete compensation, but it demonstrated that the NCAA's policies would no longer be insulated from meaningful judicial review.

Justice Brett Kavanaugh's concurring opinion further emphasized this changing legal landscape. Although he agreed entirely with the Court's unanimous decision, he questioned whether many of the NCAA's remaining compensation restrictions could survive future antitrust challenges. His opinion suggested that organizations should not expect exemption from ordinary competition laws simply because they have historically operated under a unique business model. While not legally binding, his observations have influenced ongoing discussions regarding athlete compensation and future litigation involving the NCAA.

The decision also contributed to broader reforms occurring throughout college athletics. Shortly after Alston, the NCAA adopted policies allowing student-athletes to profit from their name, image, and likeness (NIL). Although the NIL changes resulted from separate legal and political developments, Alston reinforced the growing recognition that college athletics was entering a new regulatory era. Universities, athletic conferences, and policymakers increasingly acknowledge that the traditional model of amateurism must evolve alongside the economic realities of modern collegiate sports.

Ultimately, NCAA v. Alston represents more than a victory for the student-athletes who brought the lawsuit. It reaffirmed that competition remains a central principle of American law and that organizations exercising significant economic influence must justify restrictions that limit competitive opportunities. In doing so, the decision reshaped not only the legal framework governing college athletics but also the future relationship between educational institutions, athletic organizations, and the athletes whose participation makes collegiate sports possible.

The Future of College Athletics

The Supreme Court's decision in NCAA v. Alston did not eliminate amateurism or require universities to compensate student-athletes without limitation. Instead, the ruling established an important legal principle: the NCAA's regulations are subject to the same federal antitrust scrutiny that governs other organizations participating in interstate commerce. This distinction ensures that future NCAA policies will likely face closer judicial examination whenever they restrict competition among member institutions.

One of the most significant consequences of the decision is the precedent it establishes for future litigation. Although Alston addressed only education-related benefits, the Court's reasoning—and particularly Justice Brett Kavanaugh's concurring opinion—suggested that additional compensation restrictions could also face legal challenges. By emphasizing that the NCAA is not exempt from ordinary antitrust principles, the Court opened the door for continued debate over athlete compensation, institutional authority, and the economic structure of collegiate athletics.

The decision also reflects the increasingly complex relationship between higher education and commercial athletics. Universities continue to emphasize education as the primary purpose of collegiate sports, yet many athletic departments generate revenues comparable to professional sports organizations. As media rights, conference realignment, sponsorship agreements, and Name, Image, and Likeness (NIL) opportunities continue expanding, balancing educational values with commercial realities will remain one of the NCAA's greatest challenges.

From a legal perspective, Alston reinforces an important principle extending beyond athletics. Antitrust law exists to preserve competition and prevent organizations from imposing unnecessary restraints on markets. Even when an organization pursues legitimate objectives, those objectives must be accomplished through policies that are no more restrictive than reasonably necessary. The Rule of Reason requires courts to examine evidence rather than tradition, ensuring that longstanding practices remain legally defensible under changing economic conditions.

In my view, the Supreme Court reached the correct conclusion. The NCAA presented a reasonable concern regarding the preservation of amateur athletics, but it failed to demonstrate why restrictions on education-related benefits were essential to achieving that goal. Universities should be permitted to compete by providing additional educational opportunities to student-athletes without automatically violating NCAA policy. The Court's decision preserved competition while maintaining the educational focus of collegiate athletics.

Ultimately, NCAA v. Alston represents a turning point in the governance of college sports. Rather than dismantling the NCAA, the decision requires the organization to justify its rules under established principles of competition law. As college athletics continues evolving, the case will remain one of the most significant legal precedents shaping the relationship between universities, athletic organizations, and student-athletes for years to come.

Conclusion

NCAA v. Alston represents one of the most significant legal decisions in the modern history of collegiate athletics. Although the case focused specifically on education-related benefits, its broader importance lies in reaffirming that the NCAA is subject to the same federal antitrust laws that govern other organizations participating in interstate commerce. The Supreme Court's unanimous decision demonstrated that longstanding institutional practices cannot avoid legal scrutiny simply because they have become traditional.

The Court's application of the Rule of Reason reinforced an essential principle of competition law: restrictions on competition must be reasonably necessary to accomplish legitimate objectives. While preserving amateur athletics may be an appropriate goal, the NCAA failed to demonstrate that limiting education-related benefits represented the least restrictive method of achieving that objective. As a result, the Court concluded that the organization's policies violated the Sherman Antitrust Act.

Beyond its immediate legal impact, the decision reflects the changing economic realities of college sports. Universities and athletic conferences now operate within a multibillion-dollar industry supported by broadcasting agreements, sponsorships, ticket sales, and commercial partnerships. As collegiate athletics continues evolving, governing organizations will increasingly be required to balance educational values with competitive markets and legal accountability.

In my view, the Supreme Court reached the correct conclusion. The decision preserved the NCAA's ability to regulate collegiate athletics while making clear that such authority is not unlimited. Organizations that exercise substantial control over competitive markets must remain accountable under existing law, regardless of their historical role or institutional purpose. By reaffirming the importance of competition, fairness, and judicial oversight, NCAA v. Alston established a precedent that will continue influencing the governance of college athletics for years to come.

Rather than marking the end of amateur athletics, the decision represents the beginning of a new legal framework—one in which the NCAA must justify its regulations through sound legal reasoning rather than historical tradition alone. As future disputes over athlete compensation and institutional authority arise, NCAA v. Alston will remain one of the defining Supreme Court decisions shaping the future of collegiate sports.
